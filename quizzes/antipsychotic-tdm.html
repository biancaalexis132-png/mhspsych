Antipsychotic Plasma Level Monitoring (TDM)

10 questions
1. When should an antipsychotic blood level (trough) be drawn for accurate TDM?
A. 30 minutes after the dose (peak)
B. 2–4 hours after the dose
C. At least 12 hours after the last dose, at steady state
D. Immediately before the next dose but only after 1 week of dosing
Trough levels should be drawn at least 12 hours after the last dose AND at steady state (typically after ≥5 half-lives of stable dosing, usually 5–7 days). This provides consistent, interpretable, and comparable values. Random or peak levels are not useful for TDM.
2. What is the evidence-based therapeutic plasma level target for clozapine?
A. 100–200 ng/mL
B. 350–600 ng/mL
C. 600–800 ng/mL
D. >1000 ng/mL
The therapeutic target for clozapine is 350–600 ng/mL. Levels below 350 are associated with suboptimal clinical response. Levels above 1000 ng/mL significantly increase risk of seizures, sedation, and other toxicity. TDM is most evidence-based for clozapine, haloperidol, and olanzapine.
3. A patient on clozapine 400 mg/day has a trough level of 190 ng/mL and remains symptomatic. The MOST appropriate next step before concluding clozapine has failed is:
A. Switch to a different antipsychotic
B. Assess for non-adherence, smoking status, and drug interactions — then increase dose or address PK factors
C. Add a second antipsychotic
D. The level is therapeutic — look for other diagnoses
190 ng/mL is well below the therapeutic target of 350–600 ng/mL. Before declaring treatment failure: (1) confirm adherence, (2) ask about smoking (CYP1A2 induction lowers levels), (3) check for interactions (e.g., carbamazepine). A dose increase or addressing PK variables may achieve response without switching.
4. Which enzyme is primarily responsible for clozapine and olanzapine metabolism, and what is the most common inducer in psychiatric patients?
A. CYP2D6 — fluoxetine is the main inducer
B. CYP1A2 — cigarette smoking is the main inducer
C. CYP3A4 — carbamazepine is the main inducer
D. UGT — valproate is the main inducer
CYP1A2 is the primary enzyme for clozapine and olanzapine. Cigarette smoking (polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons) strongly induces CYP1A2, significantly reducing plasma levels. This is the most clinically relevant interaction for both agents — smoking status changes should always prompt a level check.
5. The therapeutic plasma level range for haloperidol is:
A. 1–3 ng/mL
B. 5–15 ng/mL
C. 20–60 ng/mL
D. 100–350 ng/mL
The established therapeutic range for haloperidol is 5–15 ng/mL. Levels above 15 ng/mL are associated with significantly increased EPS without additional antipsychotic benefit. In elderly patients or those with renal/hepatic impairment, lower levels (3–8 ng/mL) may be adequate.
6. Which antipsychotic's TDM is most well-established with the strongest clinical evidence base?
A. Quetiapine
B. Risperidone
C. Clozapine
D. Lurasidone
Clozapine TDM has the strongest evidence base. The therapeutic target (350–600 ng/mL) is well-validated, levels correlate with response and toxicity, and monitoring is particularly important given complex pharmacokinetics and the severity of illness treated. TDM for quetiapine has limited evidence due to its complex active metabolite.
7. A patient taking risperidone is started on paroxetine for depression. What pharmacokinetic interaction is expected?
A. Paroxetine induces CYP3A4, reducing risperidone levels
B. Paroxetine inhibits CYP2D6, increasing risperidone levels
C. No significant interaction
D. Paroxetine reduces risperidone renal clearance
Risperidone is extensively metabolized by CYP2D6 to its active metabolite paliperidone. Paroxetine and fluoxetine are potent CYP2D6 inhibitors — when added, they increase risperidone and total active moiety (risperidone + paliperidone) plasma levels, potentially increasing both therapeutic and adverse effects. Monitor for EPS and consider dose reduction.
8. TDM is particularly valuable in which clinical scenario?
A. Every patient on any antipsychotic as a routine screening
B. When a patient has failed multiple medications without checking drug exposure
C. Only in patients on clozapine REMS program
D. Only in pediatric patients
TDM is most valuable when a patient appears not to respond to an antipsychotic at an adequate dose. Checking the plasma level can distinguish true non-response from subtherapeutic exposure (non-adherence, rapid metabolism, drug interactions). This prevents unnecessary medication switches in patients who may simply need a dose increase or pharmacokinetic correction.
9. CYP2D6 poor metabolizers on risperidone will have which pharmacokinetic pattern?
A. Very low risperidone levels — may need higher doses
B. Elevated risperidone levels — may need lower doses to avoid EPS
C. No change in levels — 2D6 is not relevant for risperidone
D. Faster conversion to paliperidone with improved response
CYP2D6 poor metabolizers (approximately 5–10% of Caucasians) have reduced capacity to metabolize risperidone. This results in higher risperidone plasma levels at standard doses, increasing the risk of EPS and other adverse effects. TDM or dose adjustment is warranted in known poor metabolizers.
10. Steady state for TDM sampling is typically reached after how many half-lives?
A. 1 half-life
B. 2 half-lives
C. 5 half-lives
D. 10 half-lives
Steady-state plasma concentrations are reached after approximately 4–5 half-lives of a drug at a stable dose. For most antipsychotics, this is 5–7 days. Collecting a TDM level before steady state is reached will underestimate the true therapeutic concentration and lead to inaccurate dosing decisions.

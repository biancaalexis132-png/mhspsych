Stimulants, Cannabis & Co-occurring Disorders

10 questions
1. A 26-year-old man is brought to the ED by police after being found paranoid and agitated, believing strangers were following him. He has dilated pupils, HR 132, BP 168/100, and temperature 38.4°C. Urine drug screen is positive for cocaine. What is the MOST appropriate next step?
A. Start risperidone and schedule outpatient psychiatric follow-up
B. Admit for inpatient psychiatric stabilization and begin antipsychotic therapy
C. Supportive care, benzodiazepines for agitation, cooling if needed, and reassess after drug has cleared
D. Diagnose cocaine-induced brief psychotic disorder and prescribe quetiapine at discharge
This is acute stimulant intoxication with sympathomimetic toxidrome (tachycardia, hypertension, hyperthermia, dilated pupils) and stimulant-induced psychosis. First-line management is supportive: benzodiazepines for agitation, cooling measures, monitoring. Antipsychotics and psychiatric diagnosis should wait until the drug has cleared — stimulant psychosis often resolves rapidly with abstinence.
2. A patient presents after a 3-day methamphetamine binge. He has now been sober for 36 hours and is sleeping 16 hours per day, eating constantly, and reporting profound sadness. He denies suicidal ideation. His CIWA-Ar score is 0. What is the BEST management?
A. Start an SSRI immediately for major depressive disorder
B. Administer benzodiazepines for stimulant withdrawal
C. Supportive care with nutrition, sleep hygiene, and monitoring — reassess mood in 2–4 weeks before diagnosing depression
D. Initiate buprenorphine to manage stimulant withdrawal
Stimulant withdrawal ('crash') produces hypersomnia, hyperphagia, fatigue, and dysphoria — symptoms that mirror major depression. No pharmacotherapy is FDA-approved for stimulant withdrawal. The correct approach is supportive care and watchful waiting: if depressive symptoms persist beyond 2–4 weeks of abstinence, then evaluate for primary MDD. Starting antidepressants acutely risks treating a substance-induced syndrome unnecessarily.
3. In the integrated dual diagnosis treatment model, which approach has the STRONGEST evidence base for patients with co-occurring substance use and psychiatric disorders?
A. Treat the substance use disorder first, then address the psychiatric disorder once sobriety is achieved
B. Treat psychiatric disorders only — substance use will resolve as mental illness improves
C. Treat both disorders simultaneously in an integrated setting
D. Refer all dual diagnosis patients to inpatient detox before any psychiatric treatment
Integrated dual diagnosis treatment — addressing both disorders simultaneously in the same setting — has the strongest evidence base. The sequential model ('treat one then the other') leads to poor outcomes for both conditions. Patients rarely achieve sobriety waiting for psychiatric care, and psychiatric disorders rarely resolve without substance treatment.
4. A 29-year-old woman with heavy methamphetamine use presents with paranoid delusions and auditory hallucinations. Which features would suggest stimulant-INDUCED psychosis rather than primary schizophrenia? Select all that apply.
A. Rapid improvement within days of abstinence
B. Tachycardia and dilated pupils at presentation
C. Onset in early adolescence before any drug use
D. Positive urine drug screen for methamphetamine
E. Family history of schizophrenia
Stimulant-induced psychosis is suggested by: rapid improvement with abstinence (hours-days), concurrent sympathomimetic signs (tachycardia, dilated pupils), and a positive UDS for stimulants. Early adolescent onset before drug use and family history of schizophrenia suggest primary psychotic disorder. The distinction often requires longitudinal observation.
5. The AUDIT (Alcohol Use Disorders Identification Test) is best used as:
A. A diagnostic instrument that confirms alcohol use disorder when positive
B. A screening tool to identify hazardous, harmful, and dependent drinking requiring further assessment
C. A severity scale equivalent to the CIWA-Ar for guiding benzodiazepine dosing
D. A forensic tool to determine legal intoxication
The AUDIT is a 10-item validated screening tool developed by the WHO to identify hazardous and harmful alcohol consumption and probable alcohol dependence. It is not a diagnostic instrument — a positive screen requires clinical assessment to confirm AUD. CIWA-Ar is a separate instrument for withdrawal severity.
6. Cannabis use disorder is characterized by which pattern?
A. Only occurs with daily use — occasional use cannot cause dependence
B. Tolerance, withdrawal, failed attempts to cut down, continued use despite harm — same DSM-5 SUD criteria as other substances
C. Cannabis produces no physical withdrawal symptoms
D. Cannabis use disorder requires IV or smoked route only
Cannabis use disorder follows the same DSM-5 substance use disorder criteria as other SUDs: tolerance, withdrawal, using more than intended, failed attempts to cut down, cravings, time spent obtaining/using, and continued use despite harm. Cannabis withdrawal (anxiety, irritability, insomnia, decreased appetite, restlessness) is well-recognized and begins within 1–3 days of cessation.
7. Stimulant-induced psychosis MOST closely resembles which psychiatric condition?
A. Bipolar I disorder — manic phase
B. Paranoid schizophrenia — positive symptoms with preserved affect
C. Major depressive disorder with psychotic features
D. Delirium tremens
Stimulant-induced psychosis (cocaine, methamphetamine) classically resembles paranoid schizophrenia — prominent paranoid delusions and auditory hallucinations with a relatively preserved affect (unlike schizophrenia's flat affect). This resemblance is due to dopamine excess in mesolimbic pathways, supporting the dopamine hypothesis of psychosis.
8. 'Cocaine bugs' (formication) is an example of which type of perceptual disturbance in stimulant use?
A. Auditory hallucination
B. Tactile hallucination — sensation of bugs crawling under the skin
C. Visual hallucination
D. Olfactory hallucination
Formication ('cocaine bugs' or Magnan's sign) is a tactile hallucination — the sensation of insects crawling under or on the skin — classically associated with cocaine and methamphetamine use. It results from stimulant-induced hypersensitization of peripheral sensory neurons combined with dopaminergic paranoia. It can lead to patients scratching or cutting their skin.
9. Cannabis High-Potency THC concentrates are associated with increased risk of:
A. Cardiovascular events only
B. Psychosis, cannabis use disorder, and cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome
C. Vitamin D deficiency
D. Opioid co-use only
High-potency THC products (concentrates, 'wax,' dabs — up to 80–90% THC) are associated with significantly higher risk of: acute and persistent psychosis, cannabis use disorder, cannabinoid hyperemesis syndrome (cyclic vomiting paradoxically treated by hot showers), and adverse cognitive effects. The standard medical/recreational cannabis flower is 15–25% THC — concentrates represent a qualitatively different exposure.
10. A patient presents with racing heart, elevated BP, severe agitation, diaphoresis, and paranoid delusions after a cocaine binge. Initial management includes:
A. IV haloperidol as first-line
B. Benzodiazepines for agitation + supportive care (BP monitoring, cooling); antipsychotics for persistent psychosis
C. Naloxone IV
D. Buprenorphine for craving management
For acute stimulant intoxication with agitation and sympathomimetic toxidrome: benzodiazepines are first-line (reduce agitation, lower BP, reduce seizure risk). Antipsychotics may be added for persistent psychosis but should not be used first-line due to potential cardiovascular effects and lowering seizure threshold. Supportive care (cooling, fluids, BP monitoring) is essential. Naloxone is for opioids.

Ketamine & Esketamine (Spravato): Rapid-Acting Antidepressants

10 questions
1. Ketamine's rapid antidepressant mechanism is primarily through:
A. Serotonin reuptake inhibition
B. NMDA receptor antagonism → AMPA activation → BDNF/synaptogenesis
C. MAO inhibition
D. Dopamine partial agonism
Ketamine blocks NMDA receptors → disinhibits glutamate (activates AMPA receptors) → increases BDNF and TrkB signaling → mTOR-dependent synaptogenesis in the prefrontal cortex. This rapidly reverses synaptic deficits in depression — explaining the hours-to-days onset vs. weeks for monoaminergic agents.
2. Spravato (esketamine) is FDA-approved for which two conditions?
A. TRD and bipolar depression
B. TRD and MDD with acute suicidal ideation or behavior
C. TRD and schizophrenia
D. TRD and PTSD
Esketamine (Spravato) has two FDA-approved indications: (1) treatment-resistant depression (MDD that failed ≥2 adequate trials) and (2) MDD with acute suicidal ideation or behavior (for hospitalized adults). The second indication is novel — previously, no antidepressant had an FDA indication specifically for acute suicidality.
3. What is the Spravato dosing schedule during the 4-week induction phase?
A. Daily
B. Twice weekly
C. Once weekly
D. Every 2 weeks
The Spravato induction phase: 56 mg or 84 mg intranasally TWICE per week for 4 weeks. Followed by once-weekly for 4 weeks, then every 1–2 weeks for maintenance. Patients must self-administer in a certified healthcare setting and be observed for ≥2 hours post-dose.
4. Spravato REMS requires patients to be observed for ≥2 hours post-dose primarily for monitoring of:
A. QTc prolongation and cardiac arrhythmia
B. Dissociation, sedation, and BP changes
C. Respiratory depression
D. Anaphylaxis
The primary reasons for mandatory 2-hour post-dose monitoring: dissociation (most notable expected effect), sedation/dizziness, and blood pressure changes (elevation). These effects are expected, transient, and resolve within the observation window. Patients cannot drive on the day of treatment. This monitoring requirement is why Spravato must be administered in a certified healthcare facility.
5. Compared to IV ketamine infusion, the main advantage of intranasal esketamine (Spravato) is:
A. Superior efficacy
B. Lower cost
C. FDA approval, regulatory framework, and standardized dosing without IV access
D. No dissociation risk
Spravato is FDA-approved with a clear regulatory framework (REMS), standardized dosing, and does not require IV access. IV ketamine has more efficacy data and faster onset but lacks FDA approval for depression, is not covered by most insurance plans, and requires IV access/anesthesia support. Neither eliminates dissociation risk.
6. Ketamine has abuse potential because:
A. It activates opioid receptors causing euphoria
B. Dopaminergic reward activation via indirect mechanisms — classified Schedule III controlled substance
C. It directly blocks GABA-A receptors
D. It has no abuse potential at therapeutic doses
Ketamine is a DEA Schedule III controlled substance due to its dissociative/psychedelic effects and abuse potential. Street use ('Special K') is associated with ketamine-induced bladder/kidney damage. Dissociation and euphoria from NMDA blockade may induce reward/seeking behavior. Patients with active substance use disorders should be carefully evaluated before starting ketamine therapies.
7. The antidepressant effect of a single ketamine infusion typically lasts:
A. 24 hours only
B. Days to approximately 2 weeks in most patients
C. Permanently after a single infusion
D. 3–6 months
A single IV ketamine infusion (0.5 mg/kg over 40 minutes) typically produces antidepressant effects lasting days to approximately 1–2 weeks in most patients. This transient effect is why maintenance treatment protocols (repeated infusions or esketamine every 1–2 weeks) are required for sustained benefit in TRD.
8. The standard IV ketamine infusion dose for treatment-resistant depression is:
A. 0.1 mg/kg over 10 minutes
B. 0.5 mg/kg over 40 minutes
C. 1.0 mg/kg over 20 minutes
D. 2.0 mg/kg over 60 minutes
The most studied and most commonly used protocol for IV ketamine infusion in TRD is 0.5 mg/kg infused over 40 minutes. This dose produces robust antidepressant effects while limiting dissociation and cardiovascular side effects. Higher doses increase dissociation without additional antidepressant benefit.
9. Esketamine is the S-enantiomer of ketamine. Compared to racemic ketamine, esketamine has approximately how much greater affinity for NMDA receptors?
A. Equal affinity
B. 2–4× greater NMDA affinity
C. 10× greater affinity
D. Half the affinity
Esketamine (S-ketamine) has approximately 2–4× greater NMDA receptor affinity than R-ketamine. This is why intranasal esketamine achieves therapeutic NMDA antagonism at lower doses than would be required with racemic (R+S) ketamine. This higher potency per mg also means a smaller volume to administer intranasally.
10. For which patient would ketamine/esketamine therapy be MOST appropriate to consider?
A. Patient with first-episode depression, no prior treatment
B. Patient with MDD who failed 2 adequate antidepressant trials with active suicidal ideation requiring rapid response
C. Patient with psychotic depression as monotherapy
D. Patient with substance use disorder for rapid detox
Ketamine/esketamine is most appropriate for: TRD (≥2 failed trials) and/or acute situations requiring rapid antidepressant response (active suicidal ideation). The rapid mechanism (hours to days) is uniquely valuable when waiting 4–6 weeks for a traditional antidepressant is not safe. Active psychosis or active substance use disorder are relative to absolute contraindications.

Antidepressants

Tricyclic Antidepressants (TCAs): Legacy Agents with Specific Niches

10 questions
1. The first-line treatment for TCA overdose with QRS widening >100 ms is:
A. IV physostigmine
B. IV sodium bicarbonate
C. IV flumazenil
D. IV activated charcoal
IV sodium bicarbonate is the cornerstone of TCA overdose management. It alkalinizes serum pH, increasing protein binding and reducing free TCA. It also directly reverses sodium channel blockade (the cause of QRS widening). Target serum pH 7.45–7.55. Physostigmine is NOT recommended — it can worsen cardiac arrhythmias.
2. Which TCA is preferred in elderly patients due to fewer anticholinergic effects and less orthostasis?
A. Amitriptyline
B. Imipramine
C. Clomipramine
D. Nortriptyline
Nortriptyline (a secondary amine TCA — metabolite of amitriptyline) has fewer anticholinergic side effects and less orthostatic hypotension compared to tertiary amines. It is the preferred TCA in elderly patients when a TCA is necessary. Amitriptyline is on the Beers Criteria as a potentially inappropriate medication in elderly.
3. Which TCA has FDA approval for OCD and is the most serotonergic of the class?
A. Amitriptyline
B. Desipramine
C. Clomipramine
D. Nortriptyline
Clomipramine (Anafranil) is the most serotonergic TCA with the highest SERT affinity. It has FDA approval for OCD and is considered first-line pharmacotherapy for OCD alongside SSRIs. Its tertiary amine structure also means significant anticholinergic effects and tolerability issues.
4. TCAs are particularly useful in modern practice for which condition besides depression?
A. Schizophrenia
B. Bipolar mania
C. Neuropathic pain (diabetic neuropathy, postherpetic neuralgia)
D. OCD (as first-line treatment)
TCAs (particularly amitriptyline and nortriptyline) are first-line treatments for neuropathic pain including diabetic peripheral neuropathy and postherpetic neuralgia. They act through norepinephrine and sodium channel blockade in pain pathways. Amitriptyline is also used for migraine prophylaxis. Clomipramine (TCA) is OCD-approved but SSRIs are first-line.
5. TCA toxidrome includes the '3 Cs.' Which correctly describes them?
A. Constipation, Confusion, Coma
B. Cardiovascular toxicity (QRS widening/arrhythmia), Convulsions, Coma
C. Cholinergic crisis, Cardiomegaly, Clonus
D. Catecholamine excess, Clonus, Confusion
The 3 Cs of TCA overdose: Cardiovascular toxicity (QRS widening due to Na channel blockade, QTc prolongation, hypotension), Convulsions (seizures from sodium channel blockade and CNS effects), and Coma (CNS depression). Any patient with TCA overdose and altered mental status should be managed as a potential life-threatening emergency.
6. The therapeutic window for nortriptyline serum levels is:
A. 10–30 ng/mL
B. 50–150 ng/mL
C. 200–300 ng/mL
D. No established therapeutic window
Nortriptyline has a well-established therapeutic window of 50–150 ng/mL. Levels below 50 ng/mL are associated with suboptimal response; levels above 150 ng/mL increase toxicity risk without added benefit. This makes nortriptyline particularly amenable to TDM and is used to guide dosing optimization.
7. Tertiary amine TCAs (amitriptyline, imipramine, clomipramine) have MORE adverse effects than secondary amines because:
A. They have stronger D2 blockade
B. They have broader receptor binding — more H1, M1, and α1 activity
C. They have longer half-lives
D. They undergo more extensive first-pass metabolism
Tertiary amine TCAs have broader off-target receptor binding compared to secondary amines (nortriptyline, desipramine). They have stronger H1 (sedation, weight gain), M1 (anticholinergic effects), and α1 (orthostasis) blockade. Secondary amines are metabolites of tertiary amines with reduced off-target binding, making them more tolerable.
8. Why are TCAs rarely prescribed as first-line antidepressants today?
A. They are less effective than SSRIs
B. They are significantly more expensive than SSRIs
C. Lethality in overdose, anticholinergic burden, and cardiac toxicity — SSRIs offer equivalent efficacy with safer profile
D. They are not FDA-approved for depression
TCAs have equivalent antidepressant efficacy to SSRIs. However, their lethality in overdose (as few as 5–7 doses can be fatal), anticholinergic side effects (dry mouth, constipation, urinary retention, confusion in elderly), orthostatic hypotension, and cardiac toxicity led to SSRIs replacing them as first-line treatment. TCAs remain valuable for specific indications (neuropathic pain, OCD, TRD).
9. A patient on a TCA presents with dry mouth, blurry vision, urinary retention, and confusion. This cluster is MOST consistent with:
A. Serotonin syndrome
B. Anticholinergic toxicity from TCA
C. NMS
D. Adrenergic crisis
This is classic anticholinergic toxicity: dry mouth (decreased salivation), blurry vision (pupil dilation/cycloplegia), urinary retention (detrusor muscle inhibition), and confusion/delirium (central anticholinergic syndrome). All TCAs (especially tertiary amines) block muscarinic receptors. Management: supportive care, physostigmine for severe central toxicity.
10. TCAs are associated with cardiac toxicity through which specific mechanism?
A. Direct cardiac myopathy from long-term use
B. Fast sodium channel blockade — causes QRS widening and ventricular arrhythmias in overdose
C. Beta-adrenergic blockade causing bradycardia
D. Potassium channel blockade causing isolated QTc prolongation
TCAs block fast cardiac sodium (Na+) channels, slowing phase 0 depolarization. In overdose, this causes QRS widening (hallmark of TCA toxicity), bundle branch block, and ventricular arrhythmias. This is reversed by sodium bicarbonate (which competes at the Na channel binding site). QRS >100 ms signals serious toxicity; >160 ms indicates high risk of ventricular fibrillation.

Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome & Catatonia

10 questions
1. A 38-year-old man on haloperidol decanoate was recently admitted for a bipolar manic episode. On day 3, nurses report he has become rigid, mute, and febrile (39.8°C). His BP is 160/100, HR 118, and CK returns at 8,400 U/L. What is the MOST appropriate next step?
A. Increase haloperidol — this may be inadequate treatment of mania causing this agitated state
B. Add lorazepam to the haloperidol for sedation
C. Immediately discontinue haloperidol and transfer to ICU for supportive care
D. Obtain a brain MRI to rule out stroke before making treatment decisions
This is NMS: fever + lead-pipe rigidity + altered mental status + autonomic instability + markedly elevated CK (rhabdomyolysis). The single most critical intervention is immediately stopping the offending antipsychotic agent, followed by ICU-level supportive care. Continuing or increasing haloperidol would be dangerous.
2. How do you distinguish serotonin syndrome from NMS on physical examination?
A. NMS causes diaphoresis; serotonin syndrome does not
B. Serotonin syndrome is characterized by hyperreflexia and clonus, particularly in the lower extremities; NMS causes lead-pipe rigidity with normal or decreased reflexes
C. Both syndromes are clinically identical and can only be distinguished by checking which medication was prescribed
D. NMS has a more rapid onset than serotonin syndrome
The key distinguishing physical exam finding is neuromuscular: serotonin syndrome causes HYPERREFLEXIA and CLONUS (especially lower extremity), consistent with serotonergic excess. NMS causes severe 'lead-pipe' or cogwheel RIGIDITY with normal or decreased reflexes, consistent with dopamine blockade. Both cause hyperthermia and autonomic instability, making this distinction critical.
3. A 55-year-old woman with treatment-resistant depression presents to the inpatient unit with mutism, waxy flexibility, and staring. She will hold any posture she is placed in for prolonged periods. CIWA is 0, UDS is negative. What is the FIRST-LINE diagnostic and therapeutic intervention?
A. Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) as the most effective treatment
B. Haloperidol IM for presumed psychotic agitation
C. IV lorazepam 1–2mg as a diagnostic-therapeutic challenge
D. Brain MRI before any treatment to rule out structural cause
This patient has classic catatonia: mutism, waxy flexibility (echoflexibility), and staring. The immediate diagnostic AND therapeutic intervention is a lorazepam challenge (1–2mg IV/IM). Partial or complete resolution of features within 30 minutes confirms catatonia. Do NOT give antipsychotics to a catatonic patient before benzodiazepines — this can precipitate malignant catatonia. ECT is appropriate for benzo-refractory cases.
4. Which of the following are correct statements about NMS management? Select all that apply.
A. Stop the antipsychotic immediately
B. Benzodiazepines are contraindicated in NMS
C. ICU-level monitoring is appropriate for severe cases
D. The antipsychotic should not be restarted for at least 2 weeks after full resolution
E. Dantrolene can be used for severe hyperthermia and rigidity
Correct NMS management: stop the antipsychotic immediately, transfer to ICU for severe cases, give IV fluids for rhabdomyolysis, use dantrolene and/or bromocriptine for severe rigidity/hyperthermia, and wait at least 2 weeks before restarting any antipsychotic. Benzodiazepines ARE appropriate in NMS for agitation — they are not contraindicated.
5. A patient with treatment-resistant catatonia has not responded to adequate trials of IV lorazepam (up to 8mg/day × 5 days). Her catatonic features persist and she is now febrile. What is the next step?
A. Increase lorazepam to 12mg/day before considering other options
B. Add an antipsychotic to break the catatonic state
C. Electroconvulsive therapy (ECT) — response rates exceed 80% in benzo-refractory catatonia
D. Discharge and initiate outpatient treatment when catatonia resolves spontaneously
ECT is the treatment of choice for benzodiazepine-refractory catatonia, with response rates exceeding 80%. Malignant catatonia (catatonia with fever) is life-threatening and warrants urgent ECT referral. Adding antipsychotics can worsen or precipitate malignant catatonia. Inadequate benzo trial is possible, but 5 days at 8mg/day represents an adequate trial.
6. NMS and Serotonin Syndrome can both present with hyperthermia and altered consciousness. What clinical finding BEST distinguishes NMS?
A. NMS has bradycardia; SS has tachycardia
B. NMS: 'lead-pipe' rigidity + slow onset over days; SS: clonus/hyperreflexia/myoclonus + rapid onset hours
C. NMS causes diarrhea; SS does not
D. NMS occurs only with antipsychotics; SS only with antidepressants
Key differentiators: NMS develops gradually (days), features extreme 'lead-pipe' rigidity, markedly elevated CK, hyporeflexia, and occurs after antipsychotic exposure. Serotonin syndrome develops rapidly (hours), features clonus, hyperreflexia, myoclonus (especially lower extremity), and occurs after serotonergic drug exposure. Both share hyperthermia and altered mental status.
7. The Bush-Francis Catatonia Rating Scale (BFCRS) is used to:
A. Diagnose NMS severity
B. Score catatonic features — mutism, waxy flexibility, posturing, negativism, stereotypy, etc.
C. Grade antipsychotic side effects
D. Assess AIMS tardive dyskinesia
The Bush-Francis Catatonia Rating Scale screens for and grades catatonic signs: immobility/stupor, mutism, staring, posturing/catalepsy, waxy flexibility, withdrawal, refusal to eat/drink, echophenomena (echolalia/echopraxia), negativism, and others. It guides diagnosis and treatment response monitoring.
8. What is the first-line treatment for catatonia?
A. High-dose antipsychotics
B. Lorazepam challenge (1–2 mg IV or IM) followed by scheduled benzodiazepines
C. Electroconvulsive therapy immediately
D. Haloperidol IV
First-line treatment for catatonia is the lorazepam challenge: 1–2 mg IV or IM. Response (relaxation of rigidity, resumption of movement or speech) within 10–30 minutes confirms the diagnosis and guides ongoing treatment with scheduled benzodiazepines. ECT is reserved for benzodiazepine-refractory or malignant catatonia. Antipsychotics can WORSEN catatonia and may precipitate NMS in this context.
9. Malignant catatonia can be distinguished from other catatonia by:
A. Presence of auditory hallucinations
B. Hyperthermia, autonomic instability, and potentially fatal course — resembles NMS
C. It only occurs in children
D. Response to antipsychotics distinguishes it
Malignant catatonia features hyperthermia, severe autonomic instability (tachycardia, labile BP, diaphoresis), and extremely elevated CK — clinically overlapping with NMS. It is a medical emergency. The phenomenological overlap between malignant catatonia and NMS is intentional: both may share underlying pathophysiology involving dopaminergic collapse and subsequent autonomic dysregulation.
10. Dantrolene is used in severe NMS because it:
A. Blocks dopamine receptors, reversing the antipsychotic effect
B. Acts as a direct muscle relaxant (ryanodine receptor antagonist), reducing hyperthermia from rigidity-generated heat
C. Increases GABA tone to reverse excitotoxicity
D. Reduces CNS dopamine production
Dantrolene directly relaxes skeletal muscle by blocking ryanodine receptors (preventing Ca²⁺ release from sarcoplasmic reticulum). In NMS, extreme muscle rigidity generates substantial heat, worsening hyperthermia and rhabdomyolysis. Dantrolene reduces this heat source. It is used in severe NMS alongside benzodiazepines, supportive care, and discontinuation of the offending antipsychotic.

Acute Agitation, Psychiatric Holds & Lithium Toxicity

10 questions
1. A patient on lithium 900mg/day for bipolar I disorder reports 3 days of profuse diarrhea from a GI illness. Today she is confused and ataxic. Her lithium level returns at 2.3 mEq/L. What is the MOST appropriate management?
A. Hold lithium, give oral rehydration, and recheck level in 3 days as outpatient
B. Admit for IV fluid resuscitation, hold lithium, serial levels, monitor renal function
C. Continue lithium at half-dose until GI illness resolves
D. Administer activated charcoal to reduce absorption
Level of 2.3 mEq/L with neurological symptoms (confusion, ataxia) = moderate-to-severe lithium toxicity. Dehydration from GI illness significantly reduces renal clearance, raising the level. This requires hospital admission for IV fluid resuscitation, lithium hold, serial levels, and close monitoring of renal function. Oral rehydration as outpatient is insufficient given the neurological involvement.
2. A patient on haloperidol presents with acute agitation related to alcohol intoxication. Which IM medication should you AVOID?
A. Lorazepam 1–2mg IM for anxiolytic effect
B. IM olanzapine given simultaneously with IM lorazepam
C. Haloperidol 5mg IM as monotherapy
D. Droperidol 5mg IM for rapid effect
Concurrent IM olanzapine + IM lorazepam (or IV benzodiazepines) significantly increases the risk of severe respiratory depression and cardiopulmonary arrest. This combination is contraindicated per FDA labeling. If you need to combine an antipsychotic with a benzodiazepine for agitation, use haloperidol + lorazepam (the 'B52'), not olanzapine + lorazepam.
3. Which of the following can RAISE serum lithium levels? Select all that apply.
A. NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen)
B. Thiazide diuretics
C. ACE inhibitors
D. Dehydration and salt restriction
E. Loop diuretics (furosemide)
F. Theophylline
NSAIDs (reduce renal prostaglandins → decreased lithium clearance), thiazide diuretics (paradoxically increase lithium reabsorption in proximal tubule), ACE inhibitors, and dehydration/sodium restriction all raise lithium levels by reducing renal clearance. Loop diuretics and theophylline actually LOWER lithium levels. This is clinically critical — patients must know not to take OTC ibuprofen.
4. A patient with a known psychiatric history presents to the ED expressing a wish to kill his neighbor, with whom he has been in an ongoing dispute. He is voluntary and cooperative. He has no plan, no weapon access, and no prior violence history. He agrees to follow-up tomorrow. What is the APPROPRIATE basis for evaluating a psychiatric hold?
A. Holds are not appropriate without a specific plan and weapon access
B. Any expressed violence toward another person is sufficient for a hold without further evaluation
C. A hold requires a qualifying psychiatric condition AND specific credible danger to others — evaluate the clinical picture for both
D. The patient's cooperation and agreement to follow-up eliminates the need for further risk assessment
A psychiatric hold requires both a qualifying psychiatric condition and meeting the legal threshold for dangerousness. Patient cooperation or verbal agreement to follow up does not eliminate the need for risk assessment. However, verbal threats without plan, means, or credible history do not automatically meet the threshold. The clinician must document a full risk assessment and make a judgment about whether the specific dangerousness criteria are met.
5. Hemodialysis for lithium toxicity is most clearly indicated when:
A. The serum lithium level exceeds 1.5 mEq/L
B. The patient develops any symptoms of lithium toxicity
C. There are severe symptoms (seizures, coma) OR levels exceed 3.5–4 mEq/L, or renal failure prevents clearance
D. The patient refuses oral hydration
Hemodialysis is the definitive treatment to rapidly remove lithium in severe toxicity. Clear indications include: levels >3.5–4 mEq/L, severe neurological symptoms (seizures, coma), severe cardiac arrhythmias, and renal failure preventing adequate clearance. Mild-moderate toxicity is typically managed with IV fluids and supportive care. Dialysis can be life-saving and prevents permanent neurological damage.
6. The 'B52' psychiatric emergency regimen consists of:
A. Benztropine 5 mg + Haldol 2 mg IM
B. Benadryl (diphenhydramine) 50 mg + Haldol 5 mg IM + Ativan (lorazepam) 2 mg IM
C. Bupropion 500 mg + diazepam 2 mg
D. B12 injection + haloperidol 2 mg
The 'B52' is a classic emergency psychiatry combination: Benadryl (diphenhydramine) 50 mg + Haloperidol 5 mg + Ativan (lorazepam) 2 mg — all given IM. It combines the anticholinergic-H1 sedation (diphenhydramine), D2 antipsychotic (haloperidol), and GABA-mediated sedation (lorazepam) for rapid control of acute agitation. Named for the total mg (50 + 2 = B52).
7. A 5150 psychiatric hold (California) or equivalent involuntary hold in most US states is justified when a patient is:
A. Non-compliant with outpatient medication
B. A danger to self, danger to others, OR gravely disabled due to mental disorder
C. Homeless and refusing shelter
D. Using illicit substances
Involuntary psychiatric holds (5150 in California, Baker Act in Florida, etc.) require that a person's mental disorder makes them: (1) dangerous to self, (2) dangerous to others, OR (3) gravely disabled (unable to provide for their basic needs of food, clothing, shelter). Non-compliance, homelessness, or substance use alone do not meet criteria.
8. Lithium toxicity presents with which classic triad?
A. Fever, rigidity, elevated CK
B. Tremor, ataxia/nystagmus, confusion (then seizures/coma in severe cases)
C. Diaphoresis, tachycardia, diarrhea
D. Hypotension, bradycardia, hypothermia
Lithium toxicity classically presents with a progressive triad: (1) tremor (coarse, different from therapeutic fine tremor), (2) ataxia and nystagmus, (3) confusion/delirium — progressing to seizures and coma with severe toxicity. Key triggers: dehydration, NSAIDs, thiazide diuretics (reduce renal lithium clearance), ACE inhibitors, and salt restriction.
9. Verbal de-escalation in acute agitation should be attempted BEFORE restraints or medications when:
A. Never — medications are always first-line
B. When the patient is communicative and not imminently dangerous to staff or self
C. Only in outpatient settings
D. Only when family members are present
Verbal de-escalation is always attempted first when safe to do so — when the patient is communicable and not posing immediate imminent danger. Core techniques: calm tone, maintaining distance, active listening, avoiding confrontation, offering choices, and validating emotions. Joint Commission and SAMHSA guidelines emphasize de-escalation to reduce restraint use.
10. What is the key advantage of droperidol over haloperidol for acute agitation management?
A. No QTc risk
B. Faster onset of action (IM peak effect ~10–15 minutes vs. 30–40 minutes for haloperidol)
C. Droperidol can be given orally
D. No EPS risk with droperidol
Droperidol has a faster onset of action than haloperidol IM (~10–15 minutes vs. 30–40 minutes), making it particularly useful for acute agitation requiring rapid control. Both can prolong QTc (droperidol has an FDA black-box warning for QTc, though this is considered controversial). Droperidol also has antiemetic properties.

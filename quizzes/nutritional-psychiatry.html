Nutritional Psychiatry: Food as Medicine

10 questions
1. The Antidepressant Food Score (LaChance & Ramsey) identified 12 antidepressant nutrients. Which of the following is NOT among them?
A. EPA and DHA
B. Folate and zinc
C. Lactose and sucrose
D. Iron and magnesium
The 12 antidepressant nutrients in the Antidepressant Food Score are: EPA, DHA, folate, iron, zinc, magnesium, potassium, vitamins B1, B6, B12, vitamin C, and vitamin A. These were identified based on their evidence for depression prevention or treatment. Lactose and sucrose are not included.
2. Between EPA and DHA omega-3 fatty acids, which has stronger meta-analytic evidence for antidepressant effects?
A. DHA — it is the primary structural fat in the brain
B. EPA — meta-analyses show benefit at ≥1 g EPA/day
C. They are completely equivalent
D. Neither has any evidence
EPA (eicosapentaenoic acid), not DHA, drives the antidepressant effect of omega-3 supplementation. Meta-analyses (including Sublette et al.) consistently support EPA ≥1 g/day for antidepressant effects. When recommending fish oil for depression, choose products with high EPA content (EPA:DHA ratio ≥2:1).
3. The SMILES trial demonstrated that a Mediterranean-style dietary intervention reduced MDD scores compared to social support control. The remission rate in the dietary group was approximately:
A. 5%
B. 15%
C. 32%
D. 60%
The SMILES trial (Jacka et al., 2017) showed a 32% remission rate (MADRS criteria) in the Mediterranean diet group vs. 8% in the social support control group over 12 weeks. This 4× difference was statistically and clinically significant, establishing the dietary intervention as the first RCT evidence for diet as antidepressant treatment.
4. The gut-brain axis relates to depression because:
A. Gut bacteria directly produce SSRIs
B. ~90% of serotonin is produced in the gut; microbiome diversity correlates inversely with anxiety/depression
C. The gut produces dopamine exclusively
D. Gut pH affects antidepressant absorption only
Approximately 90% of the body's serotonin is produced in the gut (by enterochromaffin cells) — though gut serotonin doesn't cross the blood-brain barrier, it influences gut motility and vagal signaling. Gut microbiome diversity inversely correlates with anxiety and depression severity, suggesting bidirectional gut-brain communication via vagal, hormonal, and immune pathways.
5. When screening a depressed patient for nutritional contributors, which lab panel is MOST clinically actionable?
A. Full amino acid panel
B. Ferritin, vitamin D (25-OH), B12, folate, TSH
C. Omega-3 index only
D. Serum zinc and copper
The most clinically impactful nutritional screen is: ferritin/iron (iron deficiency causes fatigue and depression-like symptoms), vitamin D (deficiency linked to depression risk), B12 and folate (required for neurotransmitter synthesis — deficiency causes depressive symptoms), and TSH (hypothyroidism mimics depression). These are affordable, actionable, and commonly deficient.
6. Iron deficiency (even without anemia) in psychiatry is associated with which conditions?
A. Schizophrenia and bipolar disorder
B. ADHD, restless legs syndrome, fatigue, and depression
C. OCD and PTSD only
D. No psychiatric conditions
Iron deficiency (even with normal hemoglobin — 'non-anemic iron deficiency') impairs brain function. It is associated with ADHD (dopamine synthesis requires iron), restless legs syndrome, fatigue, cognitive impairment, and depressive symptoms. Ferritin (storage iron) is a more sensitive marker than hemoglobin for functional iron deficiency in psychiatric patients.
7. The EASe-GAD trial (LaChance) examined omega-3 fatty acids in which psychiatric condition?
A. Schizophrenia
B. Generalized anxiety disorder (GAD)
C. PTSD
D. OCD
The EASe-GAD trial examined omega-3 supplementation (high EPA) for generalized anxiety disorder, finding significant reduction in anxiety severity compared to placebo. This extends the evidence for omega-3 beyond depression to anxiety disorders — consistent with the anti-inflammatory mechanism relevant to both conditions.
8. Which dietary pattern is associated with the HIGHEST risk of depression in epidemiological studies?
A. Mediterranean diet
B. DASH diet
C. Western diet (high processed food, refined sugar, trans fats)
D. Vegetarian diet
The Western dietary pattern — high in processed foods, refined carbohydrates, trans fats, and red/processed meat — is consistently associated with higher depression risk in observational studies. This pattern promotes systemic inflammation, reduces microbiome diversity, and is poor in antidepressant nutrients. The Mediterranean diet is associated with lowest depression risk.
9. Folate is important in psychiatry because it is required for:
A. Direct serotonin synthesis
B. One-carbon metabolism → SAMe production → monoamine and myelin synthesis (methylation reactions)
C. GABA receptor modulation
D. Insulin sensitivity
Folate is essential for one-carbon metabolism, producing methyltetrahydrofolate (MTHF) → homocysteine → SAMe (S-adenosylmethionine). SAMe is the universal methyl donor for monoamine synthesis (serotonin, dopamine, norepinephrine) and myelin production. Folate deficiency impairs neurotransmitter synthesis and is associated with depression, especially in patients with MTHFR gene variants.
10. L-methylfolate (Deplin) is a prescription supplement used in psychiatry as:
A. A first-line antidepressant replacing SSRIs
B. An augmentation agent for antidepressants, particularly in patients with MTHFR variants or folate deficiency
C. A mood stabilizer for bipolar disorder
D. A weight loss supplement for antipsychotic-induced obesity
L-methylfolate (the bioactive form of folate, bypassing MTHFR metabolism) is used as an augmentation agent for antidepressants. It is particularly relevant in patients with MTHFR gene variants (reduced ability to convert dietary folate to active form) or documented folate deficiency. Evidence shows improvement in antidepressant response when added to SSRIs/SNRIs.

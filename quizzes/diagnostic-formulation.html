Psychiatric Formulation & Diagnosis

10 questions
1. In the 4 P's framework, which category best describes childhood trauma that increases vulnerability to depression in adulthood?
A. Precipitating
B. Perpetuating
C. Predisposing
D. Protective
Predisposing factors are those that create vulnerability before the current episode — they increase susceptibility. Childhood trauma is a classic predisposing factor. Precipitating factors are the proximate triggers of the current episode (e.g., recent job loss). Perpetuating factors maintain the current problem (e.g., ongoing substance use). Protective factors are strengths that mitigate risk.
2. Which of the following belong in the BIOLOGICAL domain of a biopsychosocial formulation? (Select all that apply)
A. Family history of bipolar disorder
B. Current heavy alcohol use
C. Unemployment
D. Hypothyroidism
E. Negative cognitive distortions
F. Use of prednisone causing mood changes
Biological factors include genetics (family history of bipolar), substance use (alcohol), medical conditions (hypothyroidism), and medications with psychiatric effects (prednisone). Unemployment is social. Negative cognitive distortions are psychological. The biopsychosocial model helps ensure no domain is overlooked in diagnosis and treatment planning.
3. A 22-year-old presents with new psychosis. Before diagnosing schizophrenia, which should be ruled out FIRST?
A. Bipolar disorder with psychotic features
B. Medical causes including thyroid disease, CNS infection, substance use
C. Major depressive disorder with psychotic features
D. Schizoaffective disorder
First-episode psychosis requires medical workup to exclude treatable causes before attributing to a primary psychotic disorder. This includes TSH, CBC, CMP, B12, RPR/VDRL, HIV, urine drug screen, and neuroimaging in many cases. Medical causes of psychosis include thyroid disorders, autoimmune encephalitis, CNS infections, substance intoxication/withdrawal, and many others. Bipolar and MDD with psychosis are important psychiatric differential diagnoses but are ruled out after medical causes.
4. The MOST important reason to include protective factors in a psychiatric formulation is:
A. They are required by Joint Commission for documentation
B. They identify strengths that can be leveraged in treatment and affect prognosis
C. They reduce the clinician's liability if a patient harms themselves
D. They are only relevant for suicide risk assessment
Protective factors are clinically meaningful because they identify existing strengths and resources that can be leveraged in treatment. Strong social support, reasons for living, engagement with treatment, and spiritual beliefs all influence prognosis and can be emphasized and built upon therapeutically. Protective factors are relevant across all psychiatric conditions, not just suicide risk.
5. Comorbidity (more than one psychiatric diagnosis) in psychiatry is BEST described as:
A. A diagnostic error — most patients should receive only one primary diagnosis
B. Common and expected, as psychiatric conditions frequently co-occur
C. A sign that the clinician has not been sufficiently thorough in ruling out diagnoses
D. Only relevant for personality disorders
Comorbidity is the rule in psychiatry. Anxiety and depression co-occur in over 50% of cases. Substance use disorders are present in a large percentage of patients with mood and psychotic disorders. Trauma history underlies many conditions. ADHD often co-exists with mood and anxiety disorders. Recognizing and treating comorbidities is essential to effective psychiatric care.
6. For Darius (42M, second manic episode, stopped lithium 6 weeks ago), which factor is BEST categorized as 'precipitating'?
A. Bipolar I disorder (genetic predisposition)
B. Lithium discontinuation 6 weeks ago
C. Childhood with an unpredictable father
D. Unemployment and social isolation
Precipitating factors are the proximal triggers for the current episode. Lithium discontinuation is the most direct precipitant of his breakthrough mania. Genetic predisposition is predisposing. Childhood adversity is predisposing/psychological. Unemployment may be perpetuating.
7. Which of the following is a PROTECTIVE factor in Darius's formulation?
A. Prior psychiatric hospitalization
B. History of medication adherence (until 6 weeks ago)
C. Divorced
D. Unemployed
Protective factors mitigate risk and support recovery. Prior medication adherence (even if recently disrupted) suggests capacity for engagement with treatment. Hospitalization, divorce, and unemployment are risk factors or context — not protective.
8. In a biopsychosocial formulation, which of the following belong in the BIOLOGICAL domain? Select ALL that apply.
A. Genetic predisposition to bipolar disorder
B. Attachment style from childhood
C. Traumatic brain injury at age 18
D. Ongoing polysubstance use
E. Internalized shame from family stigma
Biological: genetics, neurological insults (TBI), substance use (pharmacological effects). Psychological: attachment, cognitive patterns, coping styles, internalized beliefs (shame). Social: family dynamics, housing, employment, cultural factors.
9. When writing a psychiatric formulation, which statement is MOST accurate?
A. The formulation replaces the DSM diagnosis.
B. The formulation provides a narrative explanation of why this person has this problem at this time.
C. The formulation is completed once and rarely updated.
D. The formulation is a list of diagnoses and medications.
A psychiatric formulation is a personalized, dynamic narrative that contextualizes the diagnosis — explaining the unique interplay of factors in THIS patient. It answers WHY (not just WHAT). It should be updated as new information emerges.
10. Darius asks: 'Do I really have bipolar disorder? My last doctor said it might just be depression.' How do you BEST respond?
A. "Your last doctor was wrong — you clearly have bipolar disorder."
B. "That's a fair question. Let me walk you through what distinguishes a manic episode from depression, and we can look at your history together."
C. "The diagnosis doesn't matter — what matters is that you take your medication."
D. "I agree with the other doctor — mood disorders are hard to classify."
Engaging the patient in diagnostic reasoning promotes insight, alliance, and adherence. Dismissing the previous clinician (A) damages trust. 'The diagnosis doesn't matter' (C) is clinically incorrect — it directly affects medication choice and prognosis. Agreeing without examination (D) is clinically irresponsible.

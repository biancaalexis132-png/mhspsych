Antipsychotics

Antipsychotics: Mechanisms & Classification

10 questions
1. Which dopamine pathway, when excessively blocked by antipsychotics, is responsible for extrapyramidal side effects (EPS)?
A. Mesolimbic pathway
B. Mesocortical pathway
C. Nigrostriatal pathway
D. Tuberoinfundibular pathway
Blockade of D2 receptors in the nigrostriatal pathway disrupts normal motor control, producing extrapyramidal symptoms (EPS): akathisia, acute dystonia, parkinsonism, and tardive dyskinesia. The mesolimbic pathway mediates antipsychotic efficacy. Mesocortical blockade worsens negative symptoms. Tuberoinfundibular blockade causes hyperprolactinemia.
2. Which antipsychotics have the LOWEST risk of metabolic side effects (weight gain, diabetes, dyslipidemia)? (Select all that apply)
A. Clozapine
B. Aripiprazole
C. Ziprasidone
D. Olanzapine
E. Lurasidone
F. Quetiapine
Aripiprazole, ziprasidone, and lurasidone are considered metabolically favorable — they have minimal effects on weight, glucose, and lipids. Clozapine and olanzapine carry the highest metabolic risk (weight gain, new-onset diabetes). Quetiapine has moderate-to-high metabolic risk. Metabolic monitoring (weight, waist circumference, fasting glucose, lipids) is recommended for all antipsychotics, but especially for higher-risk agents.
3. A 32-year-old with schizophrenia has failed two adequate antipsychotic trials. The MOST appropriate next step is:
A. Add a second antipsychotic (polypharmacy)
B. Initiate clozapine with appropriate monitoring
C. Switch to a long-acting injectable antipsychotic
D. Increase dose of current antipsychotic above maximum recommended range
Treatment-resistant schizophrenia is defined as failure of ≥2 adequate antipsychotic trials (different chemical classes, adequate dose, adequate duration). Clozapine is the only evidence-based treatment for treatment-resistant schizophrenia and is significantly underutilized. It requires regular CBC monitoring due to risk of agranulocytosis (1%). Polypharmacy, pushing doses above maximum, and LAIs don't address treatment resistance.
4. Risperidone causes significantly higher rates of hyperprolactinemia compared to most other second-generation antipsychotics because it:
A. Crosses the blood-brain barrier poorly
B. Has high 5-HT2A affinity
C. Does not cross the blood-brain barrier as readily, but still blocks pituitary D2 receptors outside the BBB
D. Has direct stimulating effects on pituitary lactotrophs
The pituitary tuberoinfundibular neurons sit outside the blood-brain barrier. Risperidone (and its active metabolite paliperidone) are highly hydrophilic and cross the BBB less efficiently than other SGAs, meaning relatively more drug is available to block pituitary D2 receptors — leading to high rates of prolactin elevation. Clinically, hyperprolactinemia can cause galactorrhea, amenorrhea, sexual dysfunction, and long-term osteoporosis risk.
5. Which antipsychotic is a PARTIAL D2 agonist (rather than antagonist), explaining its 'activating' side effect profile and weight-neutral properties?
A. Haloperidol
B. Olanzapine
C. Aripiprazole
D. Quetiapine
Aripiprazole (and brexpiprazole) are partial D2 agonists — they provide partial stimulation at D2 receptors rather than full blockade. In hyperdopaminergic states (mesolimbic), they reduce activity; in hypodopaminergic states (mesocortical), they maintain some activation. This partial agonism explains weight neutrality and the 'activating' side effect (agitation, insomnia, akathisia at higher doses). Full D2 antagonism characterizes haloperidol, olanzapine, and quetiapine.
6. Daniel (22M, first-episode psychosis) is started on risperidone. His father asks why not haloperidol — 'it's old and proven.' What is the MOST accurate response?
A. Haloperidol is equally effective with fewer side effects.
B. SGAs like risperidone are preferred for first-episode psychosis due to lower EPS risk, better adherence, and comparable efficacy.
C. Haloperidol is preferred because it's cheaper.
D. Haloperidol has better evidence for first-episode psychosis.
SGAs are preferred for FEP due to significantly lower EPS (dystonia, parkinsonism, tardive dyskinesia) rates and generally better tolerability. CATIE showed comparable efficacy to FGAs, and adherence is higher with SGAs. Cost matters but should be secondary to tolerability in a young patient starting their first medication.
7. Which antipsychotics have the HIGHEST risk of QTc prolongation? Select ALL that apply.
A. Ziprasidone
B. Clozapine
C. Thioridazine
D. Aripiprazole
E. Haloperidol IV
Highest QTc risk: thioridazine (now restricted), ziprasidone, IV haloperidol. Moderate: quetiapine. Low: aripiprazole, lurasidone, olanzapine. Always check baseline ECG and concurrent QT-prolonging medications before starting.
8. Which antipsychotic mechanism is responsible for the HYPERPROLACTINEMIA seen with risperidone?
A. D1 blockade in the prefrontal cortex
B. D2 blockade in the tuberoinfundibular pathway
C. H1 blockade causing hypothalamic dysfunction
D. 5-HT2A blockade in the anterior pituitary
D2 blockade in the tuberoinfundibular pathway (hypothalamus → pituitary) inhibits dopamine's normal suppression of prolactin release. Elevated prolactin → amenorrhea, galactorrhea, sexual dysfunction, bone density loss (long-term). Aripiprazole (partial D2 agonist) actually lowers prolactin — useful for risperidone-induced hyperprolactinemia.
9. A patient with treatment-resistant schizophrenia (failed 2 adequate antipsychotic trials) should be considered for:
A. Adding a second antipsychotic (polypharmacy)
B. Clozapine
C. Higher dose of current antipsychotic
D. ECT
Clozapine is the only FDA-approved medication for treatment-resistant schizophrenia (TRS), defined as failure of ≥2 adequate antipsychotic trials. It reduces positive symptoms, negative symptoms, and suicide risk. The barrier is its monitoring requirement (REMS program, weekly CBC).
10. A 35-year-old on aripiprazole reports new pathological gambling and hypersexuality since starting the medication. What is the MOST appropriate next step?
A. Refer to psychiatry for behavioral therapy for gambling disorder.
B. Reduce the aripiprazole dose or switch to a different antipsychotic.
C. Add naltrexone for impulse control.
D. Reassure the patient that this is unrelated to medication.
Aripiprazole (partial D2 agonist) can cause impulse control disorders — gambling, hypersexuality, compulsive eating — by partial dopamine agonism in reward pathways. This is a class effect of dopamine partial agonists (also seen with pramipexole in Parkinson's). The FDA added a black box warning in 2016. Dose reduction or discontinuation typically resolves the behavior.

Antidepressants

SSRIs: The Backbone of Antidepressant Therapy

10 questions
1. Which SSRI has the LONGEST half-life, resulting in the lowest risk of discontinuation syndrome?
A. Paroxetine
B. Sertraline
C. Fluoxetine
D. Citalopram
Fluoxetine has a half-life of 4–6 days, and its active metabolite norfluoxetine has a half-life of ~16 days. This self-tapering effect essentially eliminates discontinuation syndrome. Paroxetine has the shortest half-life and the HIGHEST risk of discontinuation syndrome.
2. What is the FINISH mnemonic for antidepressant discontinuation syndrome?
A. Fatigue, Insomnia, Nausea, Seizures, Hallucinations
B. Flu-like symptoms, Insomnia, Nausea, Imbalance, Sensory disturbances, Hyperarousal
C. Fever, Irritability, Numbness, Sweating, Headache
D. Fatigue, Insomnia, Nervousness, Sedation, Hypotension
FINISH: Flu-like symptoms, Insomnia, Nausea, Imbalance (dizziness, ataxia), Sensory disturbances (paresthesias, 'brain zaps'), Hyperarousal (anxiety, irritability). These symptoms can begin within 1–3 days of abrupt SSRI discontinuation and can be severe, especially with paroxetine.
3. Citalopram has an FDA maximum dose of 40 mg/day (20 mg in elderly). The reason for this restriction is:
A. Hepatotoxicity at high doses
B. Dose-dependent QTc prolongation risk
C. Renal accumulation causing nephrotoxicity
D. Risk of serotonin syndrome at higher doses
The FDA issued a safety communication limiting citalopram to 40 mg/day maximum (20 mg/day in patients >60 years, CYP2C19 poor metabolizers, or hepatic impairment) due to dose-dependent QTc prolongation. This is unique to citalopram and its S-enantiomer escitalopram (though to a lesser extent). Baseline ECG is recommended at high doses.
4. Which SSRI is a potent CYP1A2 AND CYP3A4 inhibitor and is primarily indicated for OCD rather than MDD?
A. Sertraline
B. Fluvoxamine
C. Fluoxetine
D. Paroxetine
Fluvoxamine is a potent inhibitor of CYP1A2 and CYP3A4. This creates significant drug interactions — it dramatically raises clozapine, theophylline, warfarin, and many other drug levels. It is primarily FDA-approved for OCD (and social anxiety) rather than MDD, unlike other SSRIs.
5. SSRI-related sexual dysfunction (anorgasmia, decreased libido) affects approximately what percentage of patients?
A. 5–10%
B. 20–30%
C. 50–70%
D. >90%
SSRI-induced sexual dysfunction affects an estimated 50–70% of patients (though often underreported). It includes anorgasmia, delayed ejaculation, decreased libido, and arousal difficulties. Unlike other SSRI side effects, sexual dysfunction often does NOT resolve over time. Management: dose reduction, drug holiday, switching to bupropion, or augmentation with bupropion.
6. Linezolid (an antibiotic used for MRSA) is combined with an SSRI. The primary concern is:
A. Linezolid reduces SSRI levels via CYP3A4 induction
B. Linezolid is a weak MAOI — risk of serotonin syndrome with SSRIs
C. Additive QTc prolongation
D. No clinically significant interaction
Linezolid inhibits monoamine oxidase as part of its antibiotic mechanism. When combined with SSRIs, serotonin syndrome can occur. If linezolid is essential and the SSRI cannot be safely discontinued, psychiatric consultation and hospitalization may be required. This interaction is often missed by non-psychiatrists.
7. Which SSRI is considered MOST selective for SERT with the cleanest receptor profile?
A. Fluoxetine
B. Paroxetine
C. Escitalopram
D. Fluvoxamine
Escitalopram (S-citalopram) is the most selective SSRI — highest binding affinity and selectivity for SERT with minimal off-target effects. This translates to a cleaner side effect profile compared to paroxetine (anticholinergic, CYP2D6 inhibitor) or fluoxetine (sigma-1, 5-HT2C, CYP2D6 inhibitor).
8. Paroxetine is LEAST preferred in pregnancy because:
A. It causes cardiac malformations — FDA Category D; associated with fetal cardiac defects (ventricular septal defects)
B. It dramatically increases miscarriage risk
C. No SSRIs are safe in pregnancy
D. It causes neonatal persistent pulmonary hypertension
Paroxetine is FDA Category D in pregnancy due to an association with cardiac malformations (particularly ventricular septal defects) in first-trimester exposure. Fluoxetine and sertraline have the most pregnancy safety data and are generally preferred when an SSRI is required during pregnancy.
9. Which SSRI has the broadest FDA-approved indications including MDD, OCD, bulimia nervosa, and panic disorder?
A. Sertraline
B. Escitalopram
C. Fluoxetine
D. Citalopram
Fluoxetine has the broadest FDA indications: MDD, OCD, bulimia nervosa, panic disorder, PMDD, and bipolar depression (in combination with olanzapine/Symbyax). Sertraline has the most FDA approvals overall but doesn't include bulimia. Escitalopram is approved only for MDD and GAD.
10. An initial worsening of anxiety and agitation ('activation syndrome') is most commonly seen with which SSRI at treatment initiation?
A. Paroxetine
B. Fluoxetine
C. Citalopram
D. Sertraline
Fluoxetine's activating/energizing profile (partly due to 5-HT2C antagonism and noradrenergic effects) can cause initial anxiety, agitation, and insomnia at treatment start. Patients should be warned that this usually resolves within 1–2 weeks. Starting at low doses (10 mg) and dosing in the morning can minimize this.

Child & Adolescent Psychiatry: Key Differences

10 questions
1. You are starting sertraline for a 16-year-old with major depressive disorder. His parents ask about the FDA black box warning they read about. What should you tell them?
A. The black box warning means antidepressants cause suicide in teenagers — we should avoid them
B. The warning applies only to adults, not adolescents
C. Studies showed antidepressants modestly increase suicidal ideation (not completed suicide) in those under 25 — we monitor closely with weekly follow-up for the first 4 weeks
D. The black box was removed in 2010 after new evidence showed antidepressants are safe
The FDA black box warning (2004) indicates antidepressants approximately double the rate of suicidal ideation (4% vs 2%) in those under 25, but no deaths by suicide occurred in the original studies. Untreated depression carries its own suicide risk. The appropriate response is informed consent with close monitoring: weekly follow-up for 4 weeks, then every 2 weeks. The warning remains in effect.
2. Which of the following SSRIs is FDA-approved for depression in children aged 8 and older?
A. Sertraline
B. Paroxetine
C. Fluoxetine
D. Citalopram
Fluoxetine is the only SSRI FDA-approved for MDD in children aged 8 and older (also approved for OCD age 7+). Escitalopram is approved for MDD in adolescents age 12+. Sertraline is approved for OCD in children age 6+ but not specifically for MDD in the pediatric age range. Paroxetine has been associated with concerns about suicidality in adolescents and is generally avoided.
3. Per AAP guidelines, what is the FIRST-LINE treatment for ADHD in a 4-year-old with significant hyperactivity and impulsivity?
A. Methylphenidate (Ritalin) at the lowest effective dose
B. Amphetamine salts (Adderall) — better evidence in preschoolers than methylphenidate
C. Parent training in behavior management (behavioral therapy) — medication deferred unless behavioral therapy fails
D. Combination of stimulant medication and behavioral therapy simultaneously
For preschool-age children (< 6), the AAP recommends behavioral therapy (parent training in behavior management) as first-line treatment before considering medication. Evidence supports this approach for preschoolers specifically. Stimulants are reserved for cases where behavioral therapy alone is insufficient and impairment is significant. This contrasts with school-age children where combined treatment is often appropriate.
4. A 17-year-old high school junior comes in accompanied by her parents. She has been progressively withdrawing from friends, her grades dropped from A's to C's, and she mentioned to a friend that 'things look different and strange lately.' Which of the following findings would raise concern for a psychotic prodrome? Select all that apply.
A. Social withdrawal over the past 6 months
B. Declining academic performance
C. Reports that colors seem 'too bright' or objects look 'distorted'
D. Missing periods (amenorrhea) over the past 3 months
E. Suspiciousness of classmates without clear reason
Classic psychotic prodrome features in adolescents: social withdrawal, declining function (academic, occupational), perceptual disturbances (visual distortions, heightened sensory sensitivity), and suspiciousness/paranoid ideation. Amenorrhea is not a prodromal feature of psychosis (though it may be a side effect of some antipsychotics or indicate another medical issue). Early detection and referral to Coordinated Specialty Care dramatically improves outcomes.
5. What does 'informed assent' mean in the context of prescribing psychiatric medication to a 14-year-old?
A. The same as informed consent — minors can consent for themselves
B. The process of explaining the treatment to the child in age-appropriate terms and seeking their agreement, even though legal consent is provided by the parent or guardian
C. A legal requirement that applies only to medications with black box warnings
D. Assent is optional — only parental consent is needed for minors under 18
Informed assent is the process of explaining proposed treatment to a minor patient in age-appropriate terms and actively seeking their understanding and agreement — even though legal informed consent is provided by the parent or guardian. It is an ethical (and increasingly legal) standard, not just courtesy. Engaging the adolescent's voice in treatment decisions significantly improves adherence and therapeutic alliance. It is not the same as consent.
6. Which SSRI is FDA-approved for depression in both children (≥8 years) and adolescents?
A. Paroxetine
B. Citalopram
C. Fluoxetine
D. Sertraline
Fluoxetine is the only SSRI FDA-approved for major depressive disorder in children ≥8 years. It is also approved for OCD in children ≥7 years. Other SSRIs have indications for OCD (sertraline, fluvoxamine) and anxiety in pediatric populations, but fluoxetine is the gold standard for pediatric depression.
7. The FDA black-box warning for antidepressants in patients under 25 states that SSRIs:
A. Are absolutely contraindicated in patients under 25
B. May increase risk of suicidal ideation and behavior in pediatric and young adult patients — monitor closely, especially in first weeks
C. Cause actual suicide attempts in all pediatric patients
D. Require parental consent at all ages under 25
The FDA black-box warning (added 2004, updated 2007 to include up to age 25) states that antidepressants may increase the risk of suicidal ideation and behavior in children, adolescents, and young adults. This is NOT a contraindication — untreated depression has far greater suicide risk. The warning requires close monitoring, especially in the first weeks of treatment.
8. ADHD in children is primarily treated with which class of medications?
A. SSRIs
B. CNS stimulants (methylphenidate, amphetamine salts) as first-line; atomoxetine or guanfacine as non-stimulant alternatives
C. Antipsychotics
D. Benzodiazepines
First-line pharmacological treatment for ADHD in children is CNS stimulants: methylphenidate (Ritalin, Concerta) or amphetamine salts (Adderall, Vyvanse). Non-stimulant alternatives include atomoxetine (NRI), guanfacine ER (α2A agonist), and clonidine ER. Bupropion is an off-label option. Behavioral therapy combined with medication is more effective than either alone.
9. Autism spectrum disorder (ASD) pharmacological management focuses on:
A. Treating core social deficits with SSRIs
B. Treating comorbid conditions (ADHD, anxiety, irritability) — no medication treats core ASD social/communication deficits
C. Antipsychotics for all ASD patients
D. Methylphenidate as first-line for all ASD patients
There is no medication that treats the core social communication and repetitive behavior features of ASD. Pharmacotherapy targets comorbidities: risperidone and aripiprazole are FDA-approved for irritability/self-injury in ASD; SSRIs for anxiety/OCD features; stimulants for ADHD comorbidity. Behavioral interventions (ABA, social skills training) are the primary treatment.
10. Childhood-onset bipolar disorder differs from adult bipolar disorder in which key way?
A. Children have more 'classic' episodic mania with euphoria and grandiosity
B. More chronic course, mixed/dysphoric features, rapid cycling, and higher irritability rather than classic euphoric mania
C. Childhood bipolar disorder requires no medication treatment
D. Lithium cannot be used in children under 12
Pediatric bipolar disorder often presents differently from adult BD: more chronic/continuous (less episodic), predominantly irritable rather than euphoric mania, rapid cycling, and mixed features are more common. This can make diagnosis difficult (overlap with ADHD, DMDD, and other disorders). Treatment includes lithium (FDA-approved ≥12 years for mania), valproate, and second-generation antipsychotics.

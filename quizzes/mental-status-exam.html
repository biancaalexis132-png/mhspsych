Interviewing & MSE

Mental Status Examination (MSE)

10 questions
1. A patient describes their mood as 'great, the best ever,' but when their spouse unexpectedly enters, they become tearful and shout angrily before returning to euphoria within seconds. Which term BEST describes this patient's affect?
A. Constricted
B. Blunted
C. Labile
D. Incongruent
Labile affect describes rapid, unpredictable shifts in emotional expression. This pattern — quickly transitioning between euphoria, tearfulness, and anger — is characteristic of labile affect, commonly seen in mania, borderline personality disorder, and some neurological conditions. Constricted/blunted refer to reduced range; incongruent means affect does not match stated mood or thought content.
2. During an interview, a patient's responses begin coherently but gradually drift to unrelated topics without returning to the original point. This BEST describes:
A. Circumstantial thinking
B. Tangential thinking
C. Flight of ideas
D. Loose associations
Tangential thinking occurs when the patient's responses move away from the original topic and never return. Circumstantial thinking is similar but eventually returns to the point (circuitous). Flight of ideas involves rapid topic shifts with discernible connections (classic for mania). Loose associations involve illogical connections between ideas (classic for psychosis).
3. Which of the following are considered components of THOUGHT CONTENT in the MSE? (Select all that apply)
A. Suicidal ideation
B. Auditory hallucinations
C. Persecutory delusions
D. Obsessions
E. Loose associations
F. Homicidal ideation
Thought content includes WHAT the patient thinks: suicidal ideation, homicidal ideation, delusions, obsessions, phobias, and preoccupations. Auditory hallucinations are perceptions (separate MSE category). Loose associations are thought process (HOW the patient thinks). Distinguishing thought process from content and perception is a classic exam and clinical question.
4. A patient with schizophrenia says she knows she has 'a mental illness' but refuses medications because 'I'm not that sick and the pills make me feel terrible.' Her level of insight is BEST characterized as:
A. No insight — denies having an illness
B. Partial insight — acknowledges illness but denies need for treatment
C. Good insight — aware of illness and treatment needs
D. Intellectual insight — acknowledges illness but lacks emotional understanding
This patient shows partial insight: she acknowledges having a mental illness but minimizes its severity and denies the need for medication. Good insight involves accepting both the diagnosis AND the need for treatment. Intellectual insight (option D) describes knowing the 'facts' about one's illness without genuine emotional recognition — this is a more nuanced distinction but option B is most accurate here.
5. When documenting mood vs. affect, which statement is CORRECT?
A. Mood is the clinician's observation; affect is the patient's subjective report
B. Mood is the patient's subjective report; affect is the clinician's observation of emotional expression
C. Mood and affect are synonymous terms and can be used interchangeably
D. Affect refers to long-term emotional state; mood refers to momentary expression
Mood is the sustained subjective emotional state reported by the patient (documented in their own words, in quotes). Affect is the clinician's objective observation of how the patient expresses emotion during the interview — including range, intensity, stability, and congruence. They can dissociate: e.g., a patient reports mood as 'fine' but appears visibly tearful (mood-affect incongruence).
6. Priya says: 'My thoughts are so fast — they just keep jumping from one thing to another — like, I'm thinking about the president and then tennis and then my sister's wedding.' This BEST describes:
A. Circumstantiality
B. Flight of ideas
C. Loose associations
D. Tangentiality
Flight of ideas (FOI) = rapid thought stream with connections between topics that can be followed, typical of mania. Loose associations = connections cannot be followed (more severe/schizophrenia). Tangentiality = never returns to original topic. Circumstantiality = returns eventually after long detour.
7. A patient speaks slowly, with long pauses, and says only what is necessary to answer your questions. No elaboration. This is BEST described as:
A. Poverty of thought (alogia)
B. Thought blocking
C. Mutism
D. Perseveration
Alogia (poverty of thought/speech) is characterized by reduced quantity of speech with limited content — a negative symptom of schizophrenia or severe depression. Thought blocking = abrupt cessation mid-sentence. Mutism = complete absence of speech. Perseveration = repetitive return to the same idea.
8. Which of the following are components of COGNITION in the MSE? Select ALL that apply.
A. Orientation to person, place, and time
B. Short-term and long-term memory
C. Thought content (delusions, obsessions)
D. Serial 7s or spelling WORLD backwards
E. Abstract reasoning (proverbs)
Cognition in the MSE encompasses orientation, memory, concentration (serial 7s), and abstract reasoning. Thought content (delusions, obsessions) is a separate domain.
9. A patient says his mood is 'fine' but appears tearful, avoids eye contact, and speaks softly. How do you BEST document this?
A. Mood: fine; Affect: fine
B. Mood: 'fine' (patient report); Affect: dysphoric, restricted — incongruent with stated mood
C. Mood and affect: depressed
D. Affect: neutral
Mood = the patient's subjective report (always in quotes). Affect = the clinician's objective observation of emotional expression. When they don't match, document both and note the incongruence — this is clinically significant.
10. A patient with schizophrenia says: 'I know the TV controls aren't real, but I still feel like they're sending me messages.' This demonstrates:
A. Partial insight with overvalued ideas
B. Poor insight
C. Full insight
D. Ideas of reference with preserved reality testing
Ideas of reference with preserved reality testing means the patient recognizes the belief is not rational but still experiences it. This is clinically important — it suggests some capacity for meta-cognition and may affect medication adherence differently than a patient with full delusional conviction.

Lamotrigine & Carbamazepine

10 questions
1. Why is lamotrigine titrated slowly over several weeks rather than started at a therapeutic dose?
A. To avoid initial sedation and drowsiness
B. To reduce the risk of serious rash including Stevens-Johnson syndrome
C. To allow the liver to adapt to metabolism
D. To monitor for thyroid effects
Rapid titration of lamotrigine significantly increases the risk of severe cutaneous reactions including Stevens-Johnson syndrome (SJS) and toxic epidermal necrolysis (TEN). The risk is highest in the first 2 months and with co-administration of valproate (which doubles lamotrigine levels). The standard protocol (25mg/day x2wks, then increase every 2 weeks) dramatically reduces rash risk. Any new rash on lamotrigine requires discontinuation and evaluation.
2. A patient stable on carbamazepine starts an oral contraceptive. What precaution must be taken?
A. No precaution needed — carbamazepine does not affect hormonal contraceptives
B. Advise she use additional contraception — carbamazepine induces CYP3A4 and reduces OCP levels, risking contraceptive failure
C. Reduce carbamazepine dose by 50% when combined with OCPs
D. Switch from carbamazepine to valproate before starting OCPs
Carbamazepine is a potent inducer of CYP3A4 (and other CYP enzymes), significantly increasing metabolism of oral contraceptives and reducing their plasma levels. This creates a risk of contraceptive failure. Women on carbamazepine should use additional or alternative contraception (e.g., progesterone IUD, barrier methods). Other CYP3A4-inducing anticonvulsants (phenytoin, phenobarbital, oxcarbazepine) cause similar interactions.
3. For which phase of bipolar disorder does lamotrigine have the MOST evidence?
A. Acute manic episodes
B. Rapid cycling prevention
C. Bipolar depression (acute and maintenance)
D. Mixed state management
Lamotrigine is FDA-approved for bipolar I maintenance and has the most evidence for bipolar depression specifically. It is notably less effective for acute mania. Large multicenter trials (Calabrese et al.) demonstrated superior efficacy over placebo for depressive episodes in bipolar disorder. This makes it particularly valuable when bipolar depression is the predominant burden, a common clinical scenario.
4. In an Asian patient, which genetic test should be performed before initiating carbamazepine?
A. CYP2D6 genotyping
B. HLA-B*1502 allele testing
C. G6PD deficiency screening
D. TPMT genotyping
The HLA-B*1502 allele, prevalent in patients of Han Chinese, Southeast Asian, and South Asian descent (~10% of those populations), is strongly associated with carbamazepine-induced Stevens-Johnson syndrome and toxic epidermal necrolysis. FDA recommends screening for this allele before initiating carbamazepine in patients with Asian ancestry. TPMT is relevant for thiopurines; CYP2D6 for many other drugs; G6PD for certain antimalarials.
5. When starting lamotrigine in a patient already on valproate, the initial dose and titration rate should be:
A. Doubled compared to standard — valproate reduces lamotrigine levels
B. Standard — valproate does not affect lamotrigine levels
C. Halved compared to standard — valproate approximately doubles lamotrigine levels
D. Lamotrigine is contraindicated with valproate
Valproate inhibits the glucuronidation of lamotrigine, approximately doubling its plasma levels. When starting lamotrigine with concomitant valproate: start at 12.5mg/day (vs. 25mg/day standard), and titrate at half the usual rate, with a maximum target dose of ~200mg/day (vs. 400mg for monotherapy). This drug interaction is critical to prevent rash, especially SJS risk at higher lamotrigine levels.
6. Michael (27M) has a new rash 3 weeks after starting lamotrigine. Which features would MOST concern you for Stevens-Johnson Syndrome?
A. Small, pruritic, localized rash on the arm without mucosal involvement
B. Diffuse, blistering rash with mucosal involvement (oral ulcers, conjunctival injection), fever, and rapid progression
C. Mild maculopapular rash appearing 2 weeks after starting a standard titration
D. Rash limited to the face only
SJS/TEN red flags: (1) mucosal involvement (oral, ocular, genital), (2) blistering or skin detachment, (3) fever, (4) rapid progression, (5) systemic symptoms. A localized, non-blistering rash without mucosal involvement is more likely benign. When in doubt, STOP lamotrigine and evaluate urgently — SJS mortality is 5-15%, TEN 25-30%.
7. Which factor MOST increases the risk of lamotrigine-induced rash and SJS?
A. Dose above 300mg/day
B. Rapid titration (faster than recommended) or concurrent valproate
C. Age over 40
D. Male sex
Rapid titration is the most modifiable risk factor for SJS — lamotrigine MUST be titrated slowly. Valproate inhibits lamotrigine glucuronidation → dramatically increases lamotrigine levels → increased rash risk. The combination requires the lamotrigine starting dose to be halved and titration to be even slower.
8. Lamotrigine is preferred over valproate for bipolar depression in women of childbearing potential. The PRIMARY reason is:
A. Lamotrigine is more effective for bipolar depression.
B. Lamotrigine has significantly lower teratogenic risk compared to valproate.
C. Lamotrigine has no drug interactions in pregnancy.
D. Lamotrigine is cheaper than valproate.
Valproate has documented serious teratogenicity (neural tube defects, cognitive impairment in exposed offspring). Lamotrigine has a much more favorable pregnancy safety profile (small increased risk of cleft palate, but far less than valproate). This is the primary driver of the clinical preference in women of reproductive age.
9. A patient on lamotrigine 200mg/day becomes pregnant. Estrogen from oral contraceptives has been inducing lamotrigine metabolism. She stops her OCP at 8 weeks gestation. What would you expect to happen to her lamotrigine levels?
A. Levels fall — pregnancy induces lamotrigine metabolism.
B. Levels rise — loss of estrogen-mediated induction → less metabolism → higher levels.
C. Levels remain stable — OCPs have minimal effect on lamotrigine.
D. Levels fall because pregnancy increases renal excretion.
Estrogen (in OCPs) induces UGT glucuronidation enzymes → faster lamotrigine metabolism → lower levels. Stopping OCPs → less induction → higher lamotrigine levels. Additionally, during pregnancy, UGT activity increases dramatically (lower lamotrigine levels), then drops postpartum (lamotrigine levels rise again suddenly). This pharmacokinetic roller coaster requires careful monitoring.
10. What is the FDA-approved indication for lamotrigine that makes it particularly useful in bipolar disorder?
A. Acute mania
B. Maintenance therapy to prevent depressive episodes in bipolar I
C. Acute bipolar depression
D. Rapid cycling bipolar disorder
Lamotrigine is FDA-approved for MAINTENANCE of bipolar I disorder, specifically to delay the time to depressive episodes. It is NOT approved for acute mania or acute bipolar depression (despite off-label use for depression). This is clinically important: lamotrigine is not started in acute mania — it's a maintenance agent.

Mood Stabilizers

Lithium: The Gold Standard

10 questions
1. A patient on lithium 900mg/day reports taking ibuprofen for back pain for the past week. He now presents with confusion, coarse tremor, and ataxia. His lithium level is 2.4 mEq/L. The MOST appropriate next step is:
A. Increase fluid intake and reduce lithium dose by 50%
B. Hold lithium, provide aggressive IV hydration, and consider hemodialysis if no improvement
C. Switch to valproate immediately
D. Administer activated charcoal and discharge home with close follow-up
This is moderate-to-severe lithium toxicity (level 2.4 + neurological symptoms) precipitated by NSAID use (NSAIDs reduce renal lithium clearance, raising levels ~25-50%). Management: HOLD lithium, IV hydration (promotes renal clearance), and consider hemodialysis for levels >2.5 or unresponsive severe toxicity. Lithium is renally cleared, and forced diuresis with IV saline is the primary treatment.
2. Which unique benefit does lithium provide beyond mood stabilization, supported by the strongest evidence among psychiatric medications?
A. Weight reduction in bipolar disorder
B. Prevention of dementia
C. Reduction of suicide mortality
D. Prevention of medication-induced tardive dyskinesia
Lithium has the strongest evidence of any psychiatric medication for reducing suicide mortality — multiple meta-analyses show 2-3x reduction in completed suicides compared to other mood stabilizers. This antisuicidal effect may be partially independent of its mood-stabilizing properties and may relate to direct serotonergic effects. This benefit is an important consideration when choosing between mood stabilizers.
3. Which medications or situations can INCREASE lithium levels, risking toxicity? (Select all that apply)
A. NSAIDs (e.g., ibuprofen)
B. ACE inhibitors
C. Thiazide diuretics
D. Dehydration / decreased salt intake
E. Loop diuretics (furosemide)
F. Caffeine intake
Lithium levels rise with: NSAIDs (inhibit prostaglandins → ↓ renal blood flow → ↓ lithium clearance), ACE inhibitors (reduce renal perfusion and GFR), thiazide diuretics (sodium depletion → proximal tubule reabsorbs more lithium), and dehydration/salt restriction (same mechanism as thiazides). Loop diuretics cause lithium excretion (↓ levels), not toxicity. Caffeine mildly increases renal lithium excretion (reducing levels).
4. Which organ system requires the most vigilant long-term monitoring in patients on chronic lithium therapy due to direct toxicity from the drug?
A. Cardiovascular (arrhythmias)
B. Hepatic (elevated LFTs)
C. Renal (chronic kidney disease) and thyroid (hypothyroidism)
D. Pulmonary (fibrosis)
Chronic lithium therapy requires regular monitoring of renal function (BUN, creatinine — lithium causes nephrogenic diabetes insipidus and, with long-term use, tubulointerstitial nephropathy) and thyroid function (TSH — hypothyroidism develops in 20-40% with long-term use). Lithium-induced hypothyroidism is treatable with levothyroxine; dose not require stopping lithium. Hyperparathyroidism (elevated calcium) also occurs.
5. When should a lithium level be drawn to ensure an accurate 'trough' level?
A. 1 hour after the morning dose
B. 2 hours after the evening dose
C. 12 hours after the last dose
D. Immediately before the next dose, any time of day
Lithium levels should be drawn 12 hours after the last dose to obtain a standardized trough level. This 12-hour standard is used for all published target ranges (0.6-1.2 mEq/L). Drawing at other times can produce misleading peak levels or falsely elevated results. Instruct patients to skip their morning dose if going for bloodwork and take it after the draw.
6. Why did Helen's symptoms develop after starting ibuprofen?
A. NSAIDs increase lithium clearance, causing subtherapeutic levels.
B. NSAIDs inhibit prostaglandin-mediated renal blood flow → reduced GFR → reduced lithium excretion → toxicity.
C. Ibuprofen is metabolized by the same CYP enzymes as lithium.
D. NSAIDs increase lithium absorption from the GI tract.
NSAIDs inhibit cyclooxygenase → reduced prostaglandins → afferent arteriolar vasoconstriction → reduced GFR → reduced lithium renal clearance → lithium accumulation. Lithium level can rise 25-60% with concurrent NSAID use. This is one of the most clinically important drug interactions in psychiatry.
7. Which medications are KNOWN to increase lithium levels to potentially toxic concentrations? Select ALL that apply.
A. NSAIDs (ibuprofen, naproxen)
B. ACE inhibitors (lisinopril)
C. Thiazide diuretics (hydrochlorothiazide)
D. Loop diuretics (furosemide)
E. Caffeine
NSAIDs, ACE inhibitors, and thiazides all reduce renal lithium clearance → toxicity risk. Loop diuretics (furosemide) cause sodium and fluid loss → sodium-conserving mechanisms may paradoxically retain lithium, but less predictably. Caffeine (xanthine) actually increases lithium clearance slightly — lithium levels can drop when patients stop caffeine.
8. Helen's lithium level comes back at 2.1 mEq/L. She is confused and has coarse tremor. What is the IMMEDIATE management?
A. Hold next dose, recheck level in 24 hours.
B. Hold lithium, aggressive IV hydration with normal saline, monitor renal function and lithium levels q6-8h.
C. Reduce lithium dose by 50% and monitor.
D. Dialysis is required for any level >2.0 mEq/L.
Lithium >2.0 mEq/L with symptoms = significant toxicity. Treatment: stop lithium, aggressive IV NS hydration (enhances renal lithium excretion), serial lithium levels and renal function. Hemodialysis for: level >4.0 mEq/L, level >2.5 with severe symptoms (seizures, cardiac arrhythmia), or renal failure.
9. Which statement about lithium in pregnancy is MOST accurate based on current evidence?
A. Lithium is absolutely contraindicated in pregnancy — must be stopped immediately.
B. Ebstein's anomaly risk is increased with first-trimester lithium exposure but is lower than originally estimated (0.05-0.1% vs background 0.005%).
C. Lithium is the safest mood stabilizer in pregnancy.
D. Lithium has no teratogenic risk if taken after the first trimester.
Ebstein's anomaly (cardiac) risk with first-trimester lithium is real but modest — approximately 0.05-0.1% (vs. background 0.005%). The risk of untreated bipolar disorder (relapse, postpartum psychosis, poor prenatal care, self-harm) must be weighed against this risk. A collaborative decision with the patient and OB is required.
10. Which of the following renal conditions is a RELATIVE CONTRAINDICATION to lithium?
A. Stage 1 chronic kidney disease (GFR 90-100)
B. Stage 4-5 chronic kidney disease (GFR <30)
C. History of a single kidney stone
D. Hypertension without renal impairment
Lithium is renally excreted and nephrotoxic with long-term use. Stage 4-5 CKD (GFR <30) makes safe lithium use very difficult — the kidneys cannot adequately clear lithium, and the narrow therapeutic index becomes impossible to manage. Consultation with nephrology is required if considering lithium in CKD.

Antipsychotics

Fluphenazine (Prolixin): A First-Generation Standby

10 questions
1. Fluphenazine is classified as which type of antipsychotic?
A. Second-generation (atypical)
B. High-potency first-generation (typical)
C. Low-potency first-generation
D. Partial D2 agonist
Fluphenazine is a high-potency first-generation antipsychotic (FGA/typical). High potency = high D2 affinity = low doses achieve high receptor occupancy = high EPS risk. Low-potency FGAs include chlorpromazine and thioridazine, which have more H1/M1/α1 effects.
2. The dosing interval for fluphenazine decanoate LAI is typically:
A. Monthly
B. Every 3 months
C. Every 2–4 weeks
D. Weekly
Fluphenazine decanoate has a half-life of approximately 14 days, supporting an injection interval of every 2–4 weeks. This is shorter than some newer SGAs (paliperidone palmitate 3-month) but makes it one of the most cost-effective LAI options available.
3. A patient on fluphenazine develops sudden neck stiffness and upward eye deviation 2 hours post-injection. Best immediate treatment is:
A. Propranolol 20–40 mg PO
B. IM or IV benztropine 1–2 mg
C. Diazepam IV
D. Dantrolene sodium
This is acute dystonia (torticollis + oculogyric crisis). First-line treatment is anticholinergic: benztropine 1–2 mg IM/IV or diphenhydramine 25–50 mg IM/IV. Response is rapid (minutes). Propranolol treats akathisia, not dystonia. Dantrolene is for NMS/malignant hyperthermia.
4. Compared to second-generation antipsychotics, what is a KEY advantage of fluphenazine?
A. Superior efficacy for negative symptoms
B. Metabolically neutral — minimal weight gain and glucose effects
C. Lower risk of EPS
D. No QTc prolongation risk
Fluphenazine (and other high-potency FGAs) are essentially weight-neutral and do not cause the glucose dysregulation or dyslipidemia associated with olanzapine, quetiapine, or clozapine. This metabolic neutrality makes them attractive for patients with diabetes or at high metabolic risk — though EPS remains a significant concern.
5. Cumulative annual risk of tardive dyskinesia with chronic FGA use is approximately:
A. <0.1%
B. ~0.5%
C. 3–5%
D. 15–20%
The cumulative annual risk of tardive dyskinesia with first-generation antipsychotics is approximately 3–5% per year of chronic use (significantly higher in elderly patients). With second-generation antipsychotics the risk is lower (~0.5–1%/year). The AIMS scale should be performed every 6–12 months for patients on long-term FGAs.
6. Hyperprolactinemia from fluphenazine occurs because D2 blockade in which pathway?
A. Mesolimbic
B. Mesocortical
C. Nigrostriatal
D. Tuberoinfundibular
The tuberoinfundibular pathway connects the hypothalamus to the pituitary. D2 blockade in this pathway disinhibits prolactin secretion from lactotroph cells, causing hyperprolactinemia. Symptoms include galactorrhea, amenorrhea, gynecomastia, and sexual dysfunction. High-potency FGAs and risperidone carry the highest prolactin risk.
7. Which AIMS (Abnormal Involuntary Movement Scale) score finding would meet criteria for probable tardive dyskinesia?
A. Any movement score of 1 (minimal)
B. A score of ≥2 in one body area or ≥1 in two or more body areas after 3 months of antipsychotic use
C. Score of ≥5 total
D. Only if orofacial movements are present
Probable tardive dyskinesia is defined as an AIMS score of ≥2 in at least one body area OR ≥1 in two or more body areas, following at least 3 months of cumulative antipsychotic exposure. The AIMS should be administered every 6–12 months in patients on chronic antipsychotics.
8. What is the preferred treatment for tardive dyskinesia that develops in a patient on fluphenazine?
A. Add benztropine (anticholinergic)
B. VMAT2 inhibitors (valbenazine/deutetrabenazine) and consider switching to lower-EPS antipsychotic
C. Increase fluphenazine dose to suppress movement
D. Add lithium augmentation
FDA-approved treatments for tardive dyskinesia are the VMAT2 inhibitors: valbenazine (Ingrezza) and deutetrabenazine (Austedo). These reduce vesicular dopamine release, decreasing involuntary movements. Anticholinergics can actually worsen TD. Switching to a lower-EPS risk antipsychotic (e.g., clozapine) may help over time.
9. Akathisia is best described as:
A. Involuntary repetitive movements of the face and extremities
B. Subjective inner restlessness with compulsive need to move
C. Muscle rigidity and shuffling gait
D. Acute involuntary muscle contractions causing abnormal postures
Akathisia is a subjective inner sense of motor restlessness — patients feel compelled to move, often pacing or rocking. It is one of the most distressing EPS and commonly leads to non-adherence. Treatment includes dose reduction, propranolol (10–40 mg BID), or benzodiazepines. Anticholinergics are less effective for akathisia.
10. When converting from oral fluphenazine to the decanoate LAI, oral supplementation should be maintained for approximately:
A. No overlap needed — LAI is immediately effective
B. 1–2 weeks during the transition
C. 3 months
D. 6 months
A 1–2 week oral overlap is typically maintained when transitioning to fluphenazine decanoate, as therapeutic plasma levels from the LAI take several injections to reach steady state. Therapeutic levels are typically achieved after the 3rd injection.

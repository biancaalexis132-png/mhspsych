Geriatric Psychiatry: Delirium, Dementia & Beers Criteria

10 questions
1. An 80-year-old man is admitted for a urinary tract infection. On day 2, he becomes acutely confused, sees insects on the walls, and cannot state the date. He was cognitively intact at his last appointment 2 months ago. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?
A. Lewy body dementia presenting acutely
B. New-onset schizophrenia
C. Delirium secondary to urinary tract infection
D. Major neurocognitive disorder with rapidly progressive course
This is classic delirium: acute onset (hours to days), fluctuating course, visual hallucinations, disorientation in a patient with a known medical trigger (UTI) and previously intact cognition. Delirium is always secondary to a medical cause — the UTI is the precipitant. Dementia is gradual; new-onset schizophrenia at 80 is vanishingly rare.
2. A 74-year-old woman with a recent dementia diagnosis presents with new visual hallucinations (she sees children in her room), mild tremor, and her daughter reports her cognition 'comes and goes — some days she's sharp, other days barely recognizes me.' Which antipsychotic carries the HIGHEST risk in this patient?
A. Quetiapine 12.5mg at bedtime
B. Haloperidol 0.5mg
C. Risperidone 0.25mg
D. All antipsychotics carry equal risk in this patient
This patient likely has Lewy Body Dementia (visual hallucinations + parkinsonism + fluctuating cognition). In LBD, antipsychotic sensitivity is severe — even atypical antipsychotics can cause parkinsonism and fatal reactions. Haloperidol (a high-potency typical antipsychotic) carries the highest risk and is essentially contraindicated. If an antipsychotic is necessary, quetiapine at very low doses is the preferred choice due to lowest D2 binding affinity.
3. An 82-year-old woman is admitted for hip fracture. Her home medications include zolpidem 10mg nightly, amitriptyline 25mg for neuropathy, and lorazepam 0.5mg PRN for anxiety. Which of these medications appear on the AGS Beers Criteria as potentially inappropriate for elderly patients? Select all that apply.
A. Zolpidem
B. Amitriptyline
C. Lorazepam
D. Acetaminophen (her PRN analgesic)
All three psychiatric medications are on the Beers Criteria: zolpidem (falls, confusion, residual sedation), amitriptyline (strong anticholinergic burden — the most anticholinergic TCA; cognitive effects, arrhythmias), and lorazepam/all benzodiazepines (falls, cognitive impairment, excessive sedation in elderly). Acetaminophen is appropriate in elderly patients and is not on the Beers list.
4. What is the most important reason to use the LOWEST effective dose of antipsychotics for behavioral and psychological symptoms of dementia (BPSD), beyond general dose-minimization principles?
A. Antipsychotics are ineffective for BPSD at standard doses
B. FDA black box warning: atypical antipsychotics in elderly patients with dementia-related psychosis carry increased mortality risk (~1.6x) from cerebrovascular events and infection
C. Higher doses cause more vivid hallucinations in patients with dementia
D. Medicare does not reimburse antipsychotic use in dementia patients
The FDA black box warning on all atypical antipsychotics states they are not approved for dementia-related psychosis and are associated with approximately 1.6x increased risk of death (primarily from cardiovascular events and pneumonia) in elderly patients. Informed consent, documented risk-benefit discussion, and regular reassessment at 3 months are required. Non-pharmacological approaches remain first-line.
5. The hallmark clinical feature that most reliably DISTINGUISHES delirium from dementia is:
A. The presence of visual hallucinations
B. Disorientation to place
C. Acute onset with fluctuating course and markedly impaired attention
D. Behavioral disturbance and agitation
The hallmark of delirium is acute onset (hours to days) with a fluctuating course and markedly impaired attention — the inability to maintain sustained focus. Dementia is slowly progressive with relatively preserved attention until late stages. Visual hallucinations and disorientation can occur in both. Attention testing (digit span forward, 'days of the week backwards') is the most discriminating bedside test.
6. The most important initial intervention for delirium in a hospitalized elderly patient is:
A. Immediate administration of haloperidol
B. Identify and treat the underlying cause (infection, metabolic, medication toxicity, pain)
C. Restraints to prevent falls
D. Sedation with benzodiazepines
Delirium is always a symptom of an underlying medical cause — the first step is identifying and treating the precipitant: infection (UTI, pneumonia), metabolic (hyponatremia, hypoglycemia), medication toxicity (anticholinergics, opioids, benzodiazepines), pain, urinary retention, fecal impaction, etc. Non-pharmacological measures (reorientation, sleep-wake cycle, family presence) are first-line; haloperidol is reserved for dangerous agitation only.
7. The 4As Test (4AT) is used in geriatric psychiatry to screen for:
A. Dementia severity
B. Delirium — Alertness, AMT4 (abbreviated mental test), Attention, Acute change
C. Depression (Alertness, Affect, Activity, Appetite)
D. Antipsychotic side effects
The 4AT is a rapid validated delirium screening tool: (1) Alertness, (2) AMT4 (4-item abbreviated mental test), (3) Attention (months backward), (4) Acute change or fluctuating course. Score ≥4 suggests possible or probable delirium; score 1–3 suggests possible cognitive impairment. It takes <2 minutes and requires no special training.
8. Which dementia type is characterized by Lewy bodies, visual hallucinations, REM sleep behavior disorder, and parkinsonism?
A. Alzheimer's disease
B. Frontotemporal dementia
C. Vascular dementia
D. Dementia with Lewy bodies (DLB)
Dementia with Lewy bodies features: (1) fluctuating cognition with clear episodes of altered consciousness, (2) recurrent visual hallucinations (often detailed — animals, people), (3) REM sleep behavior disorder (acting out dreams), (4) spontaneous parkinsonism (distinct from PD by cognitive onset before or within 1 year of motor symptoms). Critical: DLB patients are exquisitely sensitive to antipsychotics — even 'atypical' SGAs can cause fatal adverse reactions.
9. Late-life depression (depression in patients >65) differs from younger adult depression in which way?
A. It is milder and resolves without treatment in most cases
B. Less mood complaint — more prominent cognitive, somatic, and executive dysfunction symptoms; higher suicide completion rate
C. It always has a clear precipitant
D. SSRIs are contraindicated in older adults
Late-life depression often presents atypically: less prominent sad mood, more somatic complaints, cognitive complaints (executive dysfunction, pseudodementia), withdrawal, and loss of interest. Older adults have a HIGHER suicide completion-to-attempt ratio than younger adults (more lethal methods, more isolation). SSRIs (especially escitalopram and sertraline) are first-line for late-life depression.
10. Risperidone and other antipsychotics have an FDA black-box warning for use in elderly patients with dementia because they:
A. Worsen cognitive function — accelerating dementia progression
B. Increase mortality — primarily cardiovascular events and pneumonia
C. Cause permanent tardive dyskinesia in all elderly patients
D. Cause agranulocytosis in elderly
Antipsychotics carry an FDA black-box warning for use in elderly patients with dementia-related psychosis — associated with 1.6–1.7× increased risk of death (primarily cardiovascular events and pneumonia). They are not approved for this indication and should only be used after thorough risk-benefit discussion when behavioral interventions have failed and behavior poses significant safety risk.

Antipsychotics

Extrapyramidal Side Effects (EPS) & Management

10 questions
1. A 19-year-old male receives haloperidol 5mg IM for acute agitation. Two hours later, his neck is twisted to one side and his eyes are deviated upward. He is distressed. What is the MOST appropriate immediate treatment?
A. Repeat haloperidol 5mg IM
B. Lorazepam 2mg PO
C. Benztropine 1-2mg IM or IV
D. Propranolol 20-40mg PO
This presentation — torticollis (neck twisted) and oculogyric crisis (eyes deviated upward) occurring hours after an antipsychotic — is classic acute dystonia. It is treated immediately with an anticholinergic: benztropine (Cogentin) 1-2mg IM/IV or diphenhydramine (Benadryl) 25-50mg IM/IV. Response is typically rapid. Propranolol treats akathisia. Benzodiazepines can help with agitation but not dystonia. More haloperidol would worsen EPS.
2. A patient started on risperidone 2 weeks ago reports feeling unable to sit still, an inner sense of restlessness, and pacing constantly. He says it's worse than his psychosis. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?
A. Worsening psychosis
B. Generalized anxiety disorder
C. Akathisia
D. Restless legs syndrome
Akathisia is characterized by subjective inner restlessness with an urge to move, typically developing days to weeks after starting or increasing an antipsychotic. It is frequently mistaken for anxiety or psychotic agitation, leading to dose increases that worsen the condition. First-line treatment options include reducing the antipsychotic dose, propranolol 10-40mg BID-TID, low-dose benzodiazepine, or mirtazapine.
3. A patient on haloperidol decanoate for 3 years develops repetitive lip smacking and tongue movements that persist even when you ask them to stop. Which of the following are appropriate management options? (Select all that apply)
A. Switch to clozapine
B. Increase haloperidol dose — this will suppress the movements
C. Initiate valbenazine (Ingrezza)
D. Initiate deutetrabenazine (Austedo)
E. Switch to quetiapine at lowest effective dose
F. Add benztropine — this is the first-line treatment for TD
Tardive dyskinesia (TD) presents as involuntary, repetitive movements (especially orofacial) after months-years of antipsychotic exposure. Management: (1) Switch to lowest-EPS agent: clozapine (reduces TD) or quetiapine at low doses. (2) FDA-approved VMAT2 inhibitors: valbenazine and deutetrabenazine. Increasing the antipsychotic dose may temporarily suppress movements but worsens long-term TD. Anticholinergics (benztropine) actually worsen TD.
4. Which of the following are features of Neuroleptic Malignant Syndrome (NMS)? (Select all that apply)
A. Hyperthermia
B. Lead-pipe muscle rigidity
C. Markedly elevated creatine kinase (CK)
D. Rapid onset within hours of drug exposure
E. Autonomic instability (tachycardia, labile BP, diaphoresis)
F. Clonus and hyperreflexia
NMS is characterized by the tetrad: hyperthermia, lead-pipe rigidity, altered mental status, and autonomic instability. Laboratory findings include markedly elevated CK (from muscle breakdown), leukocytosis, and myoglobinuria. NMS typically develops over days (not hours) after dopamine antagonist exposure. Clonus and hyperreflexia are characteristic of serotonin syndrome (not NMS). Immediate management: stop the offending agent, supportive care, bromocriptine/amantadine, dantrolene.
5. Which of the following BEST distinguishes NMS from serotonin syndrome?
A. Hyperthermia is present in NMS but not in serotonin syndrome
B. NMS has lead-pipe rigidity and elevated CK; serotonin syndrome has hyperreflexia/clonus and rapid onset
C. Serotonin syndrome only occurs with MAOIs, not SSRIs
D. NMS typically resolves within hours; serotonin syndrome takes weeks
NMS vs. serotonin syndrome is a classic distinction: NMS features lead-pipe rigidity, markedly elevated CK, slower onset (days), and decreased bowel sounds. Serotonin syndrome features clonus, hyperreflexia, rapid onset (hours), hyperactive bowel sounds, and tremor. Both can have hyperthermia. Serotonin syndrome occurs with any serotonergic agent (SSRIs, SNRIs, MAOIs, triptans, tramadol, linezolid). Treatment differs: NMS requires bromocriptine/dantrolene; serotonin syndrome requires cyproheptadine and/or benzodiazepines.
6. Kevin (19M) has acute dystonia after haloperidol 5mg IM. What is the IMMEDIATE treatment?
A. Benztropine 1-2mg IM or IV
B. Diphenhydramine 25mg PO
C. Propranolol 10mg PO
D. Lorazepam 1mg PO
Acute dystonia requires anticholinergic treatment — benztropine 1-2mg IM/IV or diphenhydramine 25-50mg IM/IV. Both work rapidly (5-15 min). Oral route is too slow for acute dystonia. Propranolol treats akathisia, not dystonia. Lorazepam has limited role in acute dystonia.
7. A patient on olanzapine complains of 'restlessness — I can't stop moving my legs even when I sit.' Vital signs normal. No rigidity. This MOST likely represents:
A. Tardive dyskinesia
B. Parkinsonism
C. Akathisia
D. Neuroleptic malignant syndrome
Akathisia = subjective restlessness + objective motor restlessness (pacing, fidgeting, inability to sit still). It is NOT distress from the psychiatric condition itself — clarify this history carefully. Akathisia is a leading cause of medication nonadherence and, in severe cases, has been associated with suicidality.
8. A patient on haloperidol for 8 years develops involuntary repetitive mouth movements and tongue protrusions that persist even when medication is briefly held. This is MOST consistent with:
A. Acute dystonia
B. Drug-induced parkinsonism
C. Tardive dyskinesia
D. Akathisia
Tardive dyskinesia (TD) = involuntary, repetitive, choreiform movements — typically orofacial (lip smacking, tongue protrusion, chewing). Classic triad: develops after months-years of antipsychotic use, persists after stopping, and often worsens on dose reduction. Unlike acute EPS, TD may be permanent.
9. What is the FDA-approved treatment for tardive dyskinesia?
A. Benztropine
B. Valbenazine or deutetrabenazine (VMAT2 inhibitors)
C. Propranolol
D. Clonazepam
Valbenazine (Ingrezza) and deutetrabenazine (Austedo) — VMAT2 inhibitors that deplete presynaptic dopamine — are FDA-approved for TD. They are not cures but reduce severity. Benztropine treats acute dystonia/parkinsonism. Clonazepam may offer mild symptom relief but is not FDA-approved for TD.
10. Which of the following are TRUE regarding drug-induced parkinsonism (DIP)? Select ALL that apply.
A. Typically appears within days to weeks of starting or increasing an antipsychotic
B. Presents with tremor, rigidity, and bradykinesia
C. Always resolves when the antipsychotic is stopped
D. Treated with anticholinergics (benztropine, trihexyphenidyl)
E. Occurs more commonly with high-potency FGAs
DIP typically appears early (days-weeks), is treated with anticholinergics, and is more common with high-potency FGAs. It does NOT always resolve — in some patients, especially elderly, it may unmask underlying Parkinson's disease that persists after medication discontinuation.

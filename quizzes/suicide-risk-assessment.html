Suicide Risk Assessment

10 questions
1. Which of the following is the STRONGEST single predictor of future suicide attempt?
A. Current depressed mood
B. Prior suicide attempt
C. Male sex
D. Active alcohol use disorder
Prior suicide attempt is the single strongest predictor of future attempt and completed suicide. While male sex and substance use are important risk factors, and depressed mood contributes, a history of prior attempt significantly elevates risk and is a key element of every suicide risk assessment.
2. Which of the following are MODIFIABLE (dynamic) risk factors for suicide? (Select all that apply)
A. Hopelessness
B. Access to lethal means
C. Male sex
D. Active substance use
E. Social isolation
F. Prior suicide attempt
Dynamic (modifiable) risk factors are treatment targets — addressing them can reduce risk. Hopelessness responds to therapy and pharmacotherapy. Means restriction is evidence-based. Substance treatment reduces risk. Social isolation can be addressed through therapy and social support. Male sex and prior attempt are static (non-modifiable) risk factors, though they still inform overall risk level.
3. A patient endorses passive death wish ('I wish I were dead') but denies any active plan, intent, or prior attempts. Protective factors include strong family support and religious beliefs. The MOST appropriate next step is:
A. Involuntary inpatient hospitalization
B. Discharge without follow-up — passive ideation is low risk
C. Safety planning, means restriction counseling, and close outpatient follow-up
D. Immediate medication initiation for suicidal ideation
Passive ideation without plan, intent, or prior attempts, in the presence of protective factors, generally represents lower acute risk that can be managed outpatient. The evidence-based approach includes safety planning (Stanley-Brown model), means restriction counseling, and close follow-up. Involuntary hospitalization is reserved for higher-risk presentations. Passive ideation is never simply dismissed.
4. Which psychological state is MORE predictive of near-term suicide risk than the severity of depression?
A. Guilt
B. Hopelessness
C. Anhedonia
D. Worthlessness
Hopelessness — the belief that things will never improve — is consistently identified as more predictive of near-term suicidal behavior than the overall severity of depression. Beck's hopelessness scale captures this construct. Clinically, always ask 'Do you have any hope that things could get better?' as part of risk assessment.
5. Which intervention is considered most evidence-based for reducing access to lethal means in suicidal patients?
A. Asking patients to sign a no-harm contract
B. Means restriction counseling including specific discussion of firearm access
C. Teaching distress tolerance skills
D. Prescribing a small supply of anxiolytics for crisis moments
Means restriction counseling — specifically discussing and counseling patients and families about reducing access to firearms, medications, and other lethal means — is strongly evidence-based. Studies show increasing time between impulse and access to lethal means significantly reduces suicide rates. No-harm contracts have no evidence of efficacy. Distress tolerance is valuable but not means restriction.
6. Luz says: 'I just don't see the point anymore. I'm tired of fighting.' What is your NEXT response?
A. "Tired of fighting is a sign you need more support — we'll increase your medication."
B. "Tell me more about what that feels like — when did you start feeling like the fight wasn't worth it?"
C. "Are you thinking about suicide?"
D. "Many patients feel this way — it's normal in depression."
Before jumping to direct SI assessment, briefly exploring the feeling builds rapport and elicits more honest disclosure. This doesn't mean avoiding the question — it means earning the right to ask it. Jump too quickly to 'are you suicidal?' and you may get a reflexive denial.
7. Which of the following are STATIC risk factors for suicide? Select ALL that apply.
A. Previous suicide attempts
B. Current substance intoxication
C. Male sex
D. Family history of suicide
E. Chronic pain or medical illness
Static factors are historical and don't change: prior attempts (strongest single predictor), male sex, family history, chronic pain/illness. Dynamic factors change moment-to-moment: intoxication, hopelessness, access to means, social isolation. Both inform risk formulation.
8. Luz says she has thoughts of 'just not waking up' but denies a plan or intent. How do you BEST classify this?
A. No SI — she denied active suicidal ideation
B. Passive SI — death wish without active plan or intent
C. Active SI with plan
D. Suicidal intent
Passive SI (death wish, 'wouldn't mind if I didn't wake up') is real and requires documentation and follow-up — it is NOT benign. It differs from active SI (specific ideation, plan, intent). Passive SI can rapidly transition to active ideation. Document: 'Patient endorses passive SI — wishes not to wake up — denies active ideation, plan, or intent.'
9. Which of the following is the MOST evidence-based suicide prevention intervention?
A. Safety contracting ('no-harm agreements')
B. Means restriction counseling
C. Increasing antidepressant dose
D. Weekly therapy sessions
Means restriction — reducing access to lethal methods (firearms, stockpiled medications) — is the single most evidence-based intervention for suicide prevention. No-harm contracts have no evidence of efficacy and may create false reassurance. Therapy and medications help but act more slowly.
10. After your assessment, you determine Luz requires 1:1 monitoring. Which documentation elements are ESSENTIAL in your note?
A. Risk level only
B. Risk factors present, protective factors, risk formulation, plan and rationale
C. A verbatim safety contract
D. Your clinical impression only
Medicolegally and clinically, suicide risk documentation must include: (1) risk and protective factors identified, (2) your risk formulation with reasoning, (3) the intervention and why it was chosen. 'Low/moderate/high' alone is insufficient. The standard is 'I can defend this decision in a deposition.'

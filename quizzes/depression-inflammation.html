The Inflammation Hypothesis of Depression

10 questions
1. What percentage of patients with MDD have elevated inflammatory markers (CRP, IL-6, TNF-α)?
A. ~5%
B. ~15%
C. ~30–40%
D. ~80%
Approximately 30–40% of patients with MDD demonstrate elevated inflammatory markers. This 'inflammatory phenotype' predicts poor response to standard antidepressants and may respond better to anti-inflammatory augmentation strategies. CRP is the most clinically accessible biomarker.
2. Inflammatory cytokines reduce serotonin availability through which pathway?
A. Direct SERT upregulation
B. IDO activation → kynurenine pathway → reduced tryptophan for serotonin synthesis + quinolinic acid (neurotoxin)
C. MAO-A upregulation
D. 5-HT2A receptor downregulation
Inflammatory cytokines (IL-6, TNF-α, IFN-γ) activate indoleamine 2,3-dioxygenase (IDO), which diverts tryptophan away from serotonin synthesis toward the kynurenine pathway. This produces neurotoxic quinolinic acid (an NMDA agonist) and depletes serotonin precursor availability — two pathways converging on depressive symptoms.
3. Interferon-alpha treatment for hepatitis C induces depression in what percentage of patients, providing key evidence for the inflammation hypothesis?
A. ~5%
B. ~15%
C. ~30–50%
D. ~80%
IFN-α treatment causes depression in approximately 30–50% of patients — providing some of the most compelling experimental evidence that cytokines can directly cause depressive symptoms. This 'natural experiment' showed that antidepressants (particularly SSRIs started prophylactically) could prevent or treat cytokine-induced depression.
4. The HPA axis hyperactivation in depression contributes to neuroinflammation through:
A. Glucocorticoids suppress the immune system completely
B. Chronically elevated cortisol → hippocampal atrophy (CA3) + glucocorticoid resistance → paradoxical pro-inflammatory cytokine release
C. HPA activation reduces IDO activity
D. Cortisol directly blocks SERT
Chronic HPA activation causes sustained cortisol elevation → hippocampal CA3 region atrophy and neuronal loss. Over time, glucocorticoid receptor resistance develops, paradoxically allowing pro-inflammatory cytokines to escape suppression. This creates a self-perpetuating loop: stress → cortisol → inflammation → depression → more HPA activation.
5. Which evidence-based anti-inflammatory augmentation for treatment-resistant depression with elevated CRP has the most RCT support?
A. Prednisone
B. Celecoxib (COX-2 inhibitor) augmentation or high-EPA omega-3 fatty acids
C. High-dose aspirin
D. TNF-alpha antagonists (infliximab)
Celecoxib augmentation has RCT evidence showing benefit in inflammatory-phenotype depression. High-EPA omega-3 fatty acids (≥1 g EPA/day) have meta-analytic support. There is a single positive RCT of infliximab (anti-TNF) in high-CRP TRD patients. Prednisone and aspirin lack adequate evidence and have significant risks.
6. 'Sickness behavior' produced by cytokines mirrors depression through which CNS mechanism?
A. Direct dopamine receptor blockade
B. IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α signaling in the brain causing fatigue, anhedonia, social withdrawal, and hypersomnia
C. GABA-A receptor upregulation
D. Serotonin excess in the raphe nuclei
'Sickness behavior' is an evolutionarily conserved response to infection: fatigue, anhedonia, reduced appetite, social withdrawal, hypersomnia, and increased pain sensitivity. These symptoms (identical to depressive symptoms) are triggered by IL-1β, IL-6, and TNF-α acting directly on brain circuits including the prefrontal cortex, hippocampus, and reward pathways.
7. The PATHOS-D model (Raison & Miller) proposes that depression evolved as:
A. A serotonin deficiency disease caused by modern diet
B. An adaptive response to pathogen threat (conserved sickness behavior), now misfiring in modern environments
C. A genetic mutation causing dopamine excess
D. A consequence of HPA axis overdevelopment during evolution
The PATHOS-D model proposes that modern MDD represents the misfiring of an evolutionarily adaptive 'Pathogen Host Defense' system. In ancestral environments, behaviors now recognized as depression (social withdrawal, reduced activity, appetite change) were protective during infection/injury. In modern settings without pathogenic threat, this circuit is triggered by psychosocial stressors, producing maladaptive depression.
8. Quinolinic acid, produced by the kynurenine pathway during inflammation, is neurotoxic because it:
A. Directly blocks serotonin transporters
B. Is an NMDA receptor agonist — causing excitotoxicity and hippocampal neuronal death
C. Inhibits dopamine synthesis
D. Blocks BDNF receptor signaling
Quinolinic acid is an endogenous NMDA receptor agonist produced via the IDO→kynurenine pathway during inflammatory states. Its excitotoxic effects cause hippocampal neuronal damage and contribute to the structural brain changes (hippocampal volume loss) seen in chronic depression. This partially explains why NMDA antagonists like ketamine can rapidly improve depression.
9. C-reactive protein (CRP) is the most clinically accessible inflammatory biomarker. At what CRP level might inflammatory-phenotype depression be suspected?
A. CRP >0.5 mg/L
B. CRP >3 mg/L (high-sensitivity CRP)
C. CRP >50 mg/L
D. Any elevation in CRP indicates inflammatory depression
A high-sensitivity CRP >3 mg/L has been used in research studies to identify patients with inflammatory-phenotype depression (those likely to respond poorly to monoaminergic antidepressants and potentially benefit from anti-inflammatory strategies). This threshold distinguishes low-grade chronic inflammation from acute inflammatory processes.
10. Physical illnesses most strongly associated with depression due to shared inflammatory pathways include:
A. Hypothyroidism, diabetes
B. IBD, psoriasis, SLE, obesity, and metabolic syndrome
C. Cataracts, glaucoma
D. Anemia, hypothyroidism
Conditions characterized by chronic systemic inflammation — IBD, psoriasis, rheumatoid arthritis, SLE, obesity, and metabolic syndrome — are strongly associated with increased rates of MDD. This bidirectional relationship (inflammation → depression → more inflammation) highlights the importance of integrated medical-psychiatric care.

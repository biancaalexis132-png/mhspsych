Mood Stabilizers

Valproate (VPA)

10 questions
1. A 28-year-old woman with bipolar I disorder is stable on valproate. She announces she is trying to conceive. The MOST important initial action is:
A. Continue valproate — bipolar disorder relapses are more dangerous than teratogenicity
B. Discuss valproate's teratogenicity risks and transition to a safer alternative (e.g., lamotrigine) prior to conception if clinically feasible
C. Add high-dose folic acid to prevent all teratogenic effects
D. Switch immediately to lithium — it has no teratogenic risk
Valproate is the most teratogenic mood stabilizer — it carries ~1-2% risk of neural tube defects (spina bifida) and ~10% risk of neurodevelopmental disorders including autism and cognitive impairment. It should be avoided in pregnancy if alternatives exist. The appropriate action is to discuss risks, collaboratively consider transitioning to a safer alternative (lamotrigine is generally preferred), and ensure preconception planning with folic acid. Lithium also has teratogenic concerns (Ebstein's anomaly), though recent data suggests lower risk than historically believed.
2. A patient on valproate and lamotrigine presents confused. Labs show normal LFTs but elevated ammonia. What is the MOST likely diagnosis?
A. Valproate-induced hepatotoxicity
B. Lamotrigine-induced Stevens-Johnson syndrome
C. Valproate-induced hyperammonemic encephalopathy
D. Lamotrigine overdose
Valproate can cause hyperammonemic encephalopathy through inhibition of the urea cycle, even with normal liver function tests. The encephalopathy can manifest as confusion, somnolence, and altered consciousness. Always check an ammonia level in patients on valproate with unexplained cognitive changes. Treatment includes reducing/stopping valproate and L-carnitine supplementation.
3. Valproate significantly increases levels of which co-administered medication?
A. Lithium
B. Lamotrigine
C. Aripiprazole
D. Quetiapine
Valproate inhibits glucuronidation and significantly increases lamotrigine levels by approximately 2x. When combining valproate with lamotrigine, the lamotrigine starting dose and titration rate must be halved to reduce the risk of lamotrigine-associated rash (including Stevens-Johnson syndrome). This is one of the most clinically important drug interactions in mood stabilizer prescribing.
4. Which clinical scenario is MOST appropriate for preferring valproate over lithium?
A. First manic episode in an elderly patient with no prior mood stabilizer exposure
B. Patient with suicidal ideation and treatment-resistant depression
C. Patient with rapid cycling bipolar disorder or mixed/dysphoric manic episodes
D. Patient with bipolar disorder and chronic kidney disease
Valproate (vs. lithium) has better evidence for rapid cycling bipolar disorder and mixed/dysphoric manic states. Lithium is superior for classic euphoric mania and has the strongest evidence for suicide prevention. In patients with CKD, lithium should be used cautiously (renally cleared) while valproate is metabolized hepatically. Elderly patients may tolerate valproate somewhat better than lithium due to narrower lithium therapeutic index.
5. Which of the following are known adverse effects of valproate? (Select all that apply)
A. Thrombocytopenia
B. Hepatotoxicity
C. Weight gain
D. Hair loss (alopecia)
E. Renal tubular acidosis
F. Neural tube defects in fetal exposure
Valproate's adverse effect profile includes: thrombocytopenia (CBC monitoring), hepatotoxicity (LFT monitoring — rare but can be fatal), weight gain (significant), tremor, alopecia (reversible — zinc/selenium may help), PCOS-like syndrome, pancreatitis (rare), hyperammonemia, and teratogenicity (neural tube defects). Renal tubular acidosis is not a known VPA side effect (it's associated with topiramate).
6. What is the most common congenital anomaly associated with first-trimester valproate exposure?
A. Ebstein's anomaly (cardiac)
B. Neural tube defects (spina bifida, anencephaly)
C. Cleft palate
D. Craniosynostosis
Valproate is the most teratogenic mood stabilizer. Neural tube defects (1-2% risk, vs background 0.1%) are the most common anomaly, occurring during neural tube closure (days 17-30 of gestation — often before the patient knows she's pregnant). Valproate also causes facial dysmorphia, cardiac defects, and cognitive impairment in exposed offspring.
7. Valproate is known to inhibit multiple CYP enzymes. Which commonly co-prescribed medication has PLASMA LEVELS that rise significantly when valproate is added?
A. Carbamazepine
B. Lamotrigine
C. Sertraline
D. Lithium
Valproate inhibits UGT1A4, the enzyme that glucuronidates lamotrigine. Adding valproate doubles or triples lamotrigine levels → toxicity risk (rash, dizziness, diplopia). The lamotrigine dose must be halved when valproate is added. This is one of the most clinically important pharmacokinetic interactions in psychiatry.
8. Which monitoring is required for patients on long-term valproate? Select ALL that apply.
A. Valproate level and LFTs at baseline and periodically
B. CBC (thrombocytopenia risk)
C. Renal function every 6 months
D. Weight and BMI (weight gain risk)
E. Ammonia level if encephalopathy symptoms emerge
Valproate monitoring: levels + LFTs + CBC (thrombocytopenia, neutropenia) at baseline and periodically. Weight gain is significant (average 5-10kg). Hyperammonemic encephalopathy (confusion, lethargy) requires checking ammonia — this can occur without elevated LFTs. Renal function is not specifically required for valproate.
9. A patient on valproate develops severe abdominal pain, nausea, and vomiting. Lipase returns at 4x the upper limit of normal. What is the MOST likely valproate-related complication?
A. Valproate-induced hepatotoxicity
B. Valproate-induced pancreatitis
C. Hyperammonemic encephalopathy
D. Valproate overdose
Valproate-induced pancreatitis is rare but potentially fatal. Elevated lipase + abdominal pain in a patient on valproate = stop valproate and evaluate urgently. Hepatotoxicity also occurs (elevated transaminases) but presents differently. Both are potentially life-threatening and require immediate medication discontinuation.
10. A 45-year-old woman with bipolar disorder is maintained on valproate 1000mg/day. She complains of increased hair loss over the past 4 months. The MOST appropriate management is:
A. Switch to lithium immediately — valproate is causing permanent alopecia.
B. Consider zinc and selenium supplementation; assess if dose reduction is feasible; discuss clinical significance.
C. Reassure her that this is not medication-related.
D. Add biotin 5mg daily and continue valproate without changes.
Valproate-induced alopecia is common (up to 10-12% of patients). Mechanism: valproate depletes zinc and selenium (involved in hair follicle function). Zinc/selenium supplementation may help. Hair loss is often reversible with dose reduction. Abrupt switch to lithium is not warranted without shared decision-making about risks/benefits.

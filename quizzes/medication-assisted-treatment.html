Medication-Assisted Treatment (MAT): Buprenorphine, Methadone & Naltrexone

10 questions
1. A patient with opioid use disorder wants to start buprenorphine/naloxone. She asks why the medication contains naloxone if 'it doesn't do anything.' What is the BEST explanation?
A. Naloxone potentiates the analgesic effect of buprenorphine
B. Naloxone is systemically active when taken sublingually and reverses euphoria
C. Naloxone is an abuse deterrent — it causes withdrawal if the tablet is crushed and injected, but is not bioavailable sublingually
D. Naloxone helps prevent overdose by blocking buprenorphine's respiratory depression
The naloxone in Suboxone has negligible sublingual bioavailability and does not affect the clinical effect when taken as prescribed. Its purpose is abuse deterrence: if the tablet is crushed and injected, naloxone becomes systemically active and precipitates withdrawal, discouraging IV misuse.
2. A 34-year-old man with heroin use disorder is interested in starting naltrexone (Vivitrol) injection. His last heroin use was 4 days ago. What should you do?
A. Administer the injection today to prevent relapse
B. Wait 7–10 days after last opioid use before administering naltrexone
C. Start oral naltrexone now and transition to injection in 2 weeks
D. Naloxone challenge is not necessary before naltrexone initiation
Naltrexone is a full opioid antagonist. If given while opioids are still present in the system, it will precipitate immediate, severe withdrawal. Standard guidance is to wait at least 7–10 days after last short-acting opioid use (longer for methadone). A naloxone challenge test can confirm opioid clearance before dosing.
3. A psychiatry resident wants to start a patient on methadone maintenance for opioid use disorder through the outpatient clinic. What is the regulatory constraint?
A. Methadone for OUD requires a DEA X-waiver, same as buprenorphine
B. Methadone for OUD must be dispensed through a federally licensed Opioid Treatment Program (OTP) and cannot be prescribed in a standard office setting
C. Any DEA-licensed physician can prescribe methadone for OUD from a standard clinic
D. Methadone for OUD is only available in inpatient settings
Methadone for opioid use disorder (not pain) can only be dispensed through a federally licensed Opioid Treatment Program (OTP), commonly known as a methadone clinic. This is distinct from prescribing methadone for pain, which any licensed physician can do. Standard outpatient clinics cannot prescribe methadone for OUD.
4. A patient with alcohol use disorder and compensated cirrhosis wants pharmacotherapy to reduce drinking. Which agent is SAFEST given her liver disease?
A. Disulfiram — it works by punishing drinking behavior
B. Oral naltrexone — first-line evidence base
C. Acamprosate — renally cleared with minimal hepatic metabolism
D. Topiramate — strong evidence for alcohol use disorder
Acamprosate is renally excreted with minimal hepatic metabolism, making it the safest choice in patients with liver disease. Naltrexone is hepatotoxic at high doses and should be used cautiously in liver disease. Disulfiram is contraindicated in severe liver disease and requires drinking to cause harm. Topiramate has evidence but is off-label.
5. Which of the following statements about MAT for opioid use disorder are TRUE? Select all that apply.
A. MAT reduces OUD-related mortality by approximately 50%
B. Patients on buprenorphine maintenance should be tapered off as soon as they stop using illicit opioids
C. Adequate buprenorphine dosing (typically 16–24mg/day) improves retention in treatment
D. Methadone for OUD requires QTc monitoring due to cardiac risk
E. Stopping MAT abruptly dramatically increases overdose mortality
MAT reduces OUD mortality by ~50%. Adequate dosing (16–24mg) improves retention. Methadone requires QTc monitoring. Stopping MAT — especially abruptly — significantly increases overdose mortality risk as tolerance drops. The statement about tapering off MAT after stopping illicit use is false — maintenance treatment is often long-term and should not be discontinued prematurely.
6. Buprenorphine's 'ceiling effect' on respiratory depression means:
A. Above a certain dose, respiratory depression does not increase proportionally — making it safer in overdose than full agonists
B. Buprenorphine cannot cause any respiratory depression at any dose
C. The ceiling effect limits its analgesic efficacy only
D. Buprenorphine has no ceiling effect — it is equivalent to full agonists
Buprenorphine's partial agonism creates a ceiling effect on respiratory depression — beyond a certain dose, increasing dose does not further increase respiratory depression. This makes buprenorphine significantly safer in overdose than full opioid agonists (methadone, heroin). However, respiratory depression CAN occur, especially in combination with benzodiazepines or alcohol.
7. Naltrexone for opioid use disorder works by:
A. Partial opioid agonism — similar to buprenorphine
B. Full opioid receptor antagonism — blocking opioid effects; monthly injectable (Vivitrol) improves adherence
C. Decreasing dopamine in the reward circuit
D. GABA modulation
Naltrexone is a full opioid receptor antagonist — it competitively blocks mu, kappa, and delta receptors, completely preventing opioid effects if the patient uses opioids. Monthly injectable naltrexone (Vivitrol) dramatically improves adherence compared to daily oral tablets. Unlike buprenorphine, naltrexone requires full detoxification before initiation (risk of precipitating withdrawal otherwise).
8. Why must patients be fully detoxified before starting oral naltrexone for OUD?
A. Naltrexone is hepatotoxic if opioids are still present
B. Naltrexone displaces opioids from receptors, precipitating acute severe withdrawal in dependent patients
C. Naltrexone increases opioid cravings if started too early
D. No detoxification is required — naltrexone can be started at any time
If naltrexone is given to an opioid-dependent patient who still has opioids in their system, it competitively displaces opioids from receptors, precipitating acute severe opioid withdrawal. Patients must typically be opioid-free for 7–10 days (longer for methadone) before naltrexone initiation. The urine drug screen and a naloxone challenge can help confirm adequate detoxification.
9. Methadone for OUD can ONLY be dispensed through:
A. Any pharmacy with a DEA Schedule II prescription
B. Federally regulated Opioid Treatment Programs (OTPs) — daily observed dosing initially
C. Certified physicians in office-based practices
D. Hospital inpatient units only
Methadone for OUD must be dispensed through federally regulated Opioid Treatment Programs (OTPs). These programs require daily observed dosing initially, with take-home doses granted only after demonstrated stability. This differs from buprenorphine, which can be prescribed in office-based settings. The OTP structure addresses methadone's significant overdose and diversion risks.
10. Naltrexone is ALSO FDA-approved for which other substance use disorder (other than OUD)?
A. Cocaine use disorder
B. Alcohol use disorder
C. Benzodiazepine use disorder
D. Stimulant use disorder
Naltrexone (oral and injectable Vivitrol) is FDA-approved for alcohol use disorder as well as OUD. For alcohol, it reduces the pleasurable 'reward' effects of alcohol (mu-opioid receptor antagonism in reward circuits) and reduces heavy drinking days. It is often combined with behavioral therapy and can be particularly effective in patients with strong alcohol cravings.

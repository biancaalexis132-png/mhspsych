Withdrawal Syndromes: Life-Threatening vs. Uncomfortable

10 questions
1. A 52-year-old man is admitted for cellulitis. The nurse reports he is diaphoretic, tremulous, and HR 118. His last drink was 18 hours ago. CIWA-Ar score is 22. He had a withdrawal seizure 2 years ago. What is the MOST appropriate immediate intervention?
A. Haloperidol 5mg IM for agitation
B. IV diazepam 10mg now + thiamine 100mg IV + cardiac monitoring + consider ICU transfer
C. Oral lorazepam 1mg PRN
D. Observation only and reassess in 4 hours
CIWA ≥20 + prior seizure = severe withdrawal requiring IV benzodiazepines, thiamine before any dextrose, continuous monitoring, and likely ICU-level care. Haloperidol is contraindicated (lowers seizure threshold). Oral low-dose PRN benzos are woefully inadequate here.
2. A patient with opioid use disorder (heroin) is being admitted for MAT induction with buprenorphine/naloxone. His last use was 14 hours ago. He is yawning, has gooseflesh, mild GI cramping, and a COWS score of 7. What should you do?
A. Start buprenorphine/naloxone 8mg/2mg sublingual now
B. Wait until COWS ≥ 8–12, then give first dose
C. Give methadone 30mg orally as a bridge
D. Discharge and schedule outpatient MAT initiation
COWS score of 7 is below the threshold of 8–12 recommended before buprenorphine induction. Starting too early risks precipitated withdrawal — buprenorphine displaces full agonists, triggering sudden severe withdrawal. Wait for objective withdrawal before dosing.
3. Which of the following withdrawal syndromes can be potentially FATAL? Select all that apply.
A. Alcohol withdrawal
B. Heroin withdrawal
C. Benzodiazepine withdrawal
D. Cocaine withdrawal
E. Barbiturate withdrawal
Alcohol, benzodiazepine, and barbiturate withdrawal are the three potentially fatal withdrawal syndromes — all share the mechanism of GABAergic rebound. Opioid withdrawal is extremely uncomfortable but rarely fatal in otherwise healthy adults. Cocaine withdrawal causes profound dysphoria and depression but not seizures or cardiovascular instability.
4. A 45-year-old woman with alcohol use disorder is brought to the ED confused and ataxic. She is not in withdrawal yet. The team starts D5W before calling you. What immediate intervention do you recommend?
A. Administer IV naloxone to reverse suspected opioid coinestion
B. Stop the D5W and give IV thiamine 100mg immediately
C. Obtain a brain MRI before any treatment
D. Start CIWA-Ar protocol and wait for withdrawal to emerge
This presentation (ataxia, confusion, alcohol use disorder) is classic Wernicke's encephalopathy risk. Glucose infusion in a thiamine-depleted patient can precipitate or worsen Wernicke's within minutes. Thiamine must be given BEFORE or simultaneously with dextrose. Stop the glucose and give IV thiamine immediately.
5. Which of the following is a sign of precipitated withdrawal after premature buprenorphine administration?
A. Gradual onset of mild nausea over 6–8 hours
B. Sudden onset of severe pain, profuse sweating, and agitation within 30–60 minutes of dosing
C. Respiratory depression and miosis
D. Improvement in COWS score
Precipitated withdrawal occurs when buprenorphine displaces full agonists from opioid receptors, triggering sudden, severe withdrawal symptoms within 30–60 minutes of the dose. Unlike natural withdrawal which is gradual, precipitated withdrawal is abrupt and severe. This is why waiting for COWS ≥8–12 is critical.
6. Alcohol withdrawal seizures typically occur within what time frame after last drink?
A. Within 2 hours
B. 6–48 hours
C. 3–5 days
D. 1–2 weeks
Alcohol withdrawal seizures characteristically occur 6–48 hours after last drink (peak around 24 hours). They are generalized tonic-clonic seizures. If a patient presents >48 hours after last drink with seizures, consider other etiologies. Untreated withdrawal can progress to status epilepticus.
7. Delirium tremens (DTs) is most dangerous because:
A. It causes permanent memory impairment
B. It carries 5–15% mortality if untreated — caused by autonomic instability, hyperthermia, and seizures
C. It causes irreversible hepatic failure
D. It is resistant to all benzodiazepines
Delirium tremens has historically carried 5–15% mortality if untreated. Death results from autonomic dysregulation (severe hypertension, tachycardia, hyperthermia, arrhythmia) and status epilepticus. With appropriate benzodiazepine loading and ICU-level supportive care, mortality has decreased significantly (to ~1–5%). DTs typically begin 48–96 hours after last drink.
8. The CIWA-Ar scale is used to:
A. Diagnose alcohol use disorder
B. Score alcohol withdrawal severity and guide benzodiazepine dosing
C. Measure craving for alcohol in outpatient treatment
D. Predict relapse risk after detox
The Clinical Institute Withdrawal Assessment for Alcohol-Revised (CIWA-Ar) is a 10-item scale assessing: nausea/vomiting, tremor, paroxysmal sweats, anxiety, agitation, tactile disturbances, auditory disturbances, visual disturbances, headache, and orientation. Symptom-triggered benzodiazepine dosing using CIWA-Ar reduces total benzodiazepine use and length of stay compared to fixed-schedule dosing.
9. Opioid withdrawal, while extremely uncomfortable, is rarely life-threatening in otherwise healthy adults. Which signs distinguish opioid withdrawal from alcohol withdrawal?
A. Opioid withdrawal causes seizures; alcohol does not
B. Opioid withdrawal: mydriasis, piloerection, yawning, lacrimation, diarrhea (NO seizures in healthy adults); Alcohol: potential seizures and DTs
C. Alcohol withdrawal has more GI symptoms
D. They are clinically identical
Opioid withdrawal presents with: mydriasis (pupil dilation), piloerection ('goosebumps'), yawning, rhinorrhea, lacrimation, diaphoresis, nausea/vomiting, diarrhea, and myalgias. Unlike alcohol/benzo withdrawal, seizures do not occur in opioid withdrawal in otherwise healthy adults (though they can in neonatal abstinence syndrome). This is why a detailed substance history is critical in any withdrawal presentation.
10. For a patient presenting in severe alcohol withdrawal, what is the first-line pharmacological treatment?
A. Haloperidol
B. IV or oral benzodiazepines (diazepam or lorazepam)
C. Naltrexone
D. Buprenorphine
Benzodiazepines are first-line for alcohol withdrawal — they cross-react at GABA-A receptors, substituting for alcohol's GABAergic effects and preventing seizures and DTs. Diazepam has long half-life for smoother coverage; lorazepam is preferred in hepatic impairment (LOT rule). IV phenobarbital is an alternative in refractory cases. Antipsychotics do NOT prevent withdrawal seizures.

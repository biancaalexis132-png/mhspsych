Benzodiazepines: Pharmacology & Risks

10 questions
1. A patient with alcohol dependence and cirrhosis requires benzodiazepine treatment for alcohol withdrawal. Which benzodiazepine is MOST appropriate?
A. Diazepam
B. Chlordiazepoxide
C. Lorazepam
D. Alprazolam
In patients with liver disease, lorazepam (along with oxazepam and temazepam — the 'FOLT' group) is preferred because it undergoes only glucuronidation (phase II metabolism), not phase I hepatic oxidative metabolism. Diazepam and chlordiazepoxide undergo extensive hepatic phase I metabolism, resulting in drug and metabolite accumulation in cirrhosis, increasing encephalopathy and over-sedation risk. Alprazolam's short half-life makes it problematic for withdrawal management.
2. Which benzodiazepine withdrawal complication can be FATAL and distinguishes it from most opioid withdrawal?
A. Severe anxiety and insomnia
B. Nausea and vomiting
C. Grand mal seizures and delirium
D. Muscle cramps and diaphoresis
Benzodiazepine (and alcohol) withdrawal can cause grand mal seizures and delirium tremens — both potentially fatal. This is a critical distinction: benzodiazepine withdrawal is medically serious and requires supervised management, unlike opioid withdrawal (uncomfortable but rarely fatal in otherwise healthy adults). Seizures typically occur 24-72 hours after last use. Treatment: benzodiazepines (diazepam preferred for most patients) with CIWA protocol monitoring.
3. Which benzodiazepine is preferred for ALCOHOL WITHDRAWAL due to its long half-life and active metabolites that provide a smooth, self-tapering effect?
A. Lorazepam
B. Diazepam
C. Alprazolam
D. Oxazepam
Diazepam (Valium) is often preferred for alcohol withdrawal in patients with intact liver function due to its very long half-life and active metabolites (desmethyldiazepam, oxazepam), which provide a natural taper effect that reduces breakthrough seizure risk. This self-tapering minimizes the need for frequent dosing. In patients with liver disease, lorazepam is preferred instead (hepatic metabolism impaired).
4. Which of the following are EVIDENCE-BASED non-benzodiazepine alternatives for generalized anxiety disorder? (Select all that apply)
A. Buspirone
B. SSRIs and SNRIs (e.g., sertraline, venlafaxine)
C. Hydroxyzine
D. Pregabalin (Lyrica)
E. Diphenhydramine (Benadryl)
F. Cognitive behavioral therapy (CBT)
Evidence-based non-benzodiazepine treatments for GAD: SSRIs/SNRIs (first-line pharmacotherapy), buspirone (effective but takes 2-4 weeks), hydroxyzine (antihistamine with rapid anxiolytic effect), pregabalin (approved in Europe, off-label US), and CBT (first-line psychotherapy). Diphenhydramine lacks evidence for GAD and is not recommended — tolerance develops quickly and anticholinergic effects are problematic.
5. Buspirone is being considered for a patient with GAD who previously responded well to benzodiazepines. What should the patient be counseled about buspirone's onset of action?
A. Buspirone works within 30-60 minutes like benzodiazepines
B. Buspirone requires 2-4 weeks of regular dosing before full anxiolytic benefit
C. Buspirone works best when taken only as needed (PRN)
D. Buspirone has no delay — it works through the same mechanism as benzodiazepines
Buspirone is a 5-HT1A partial agonist with a delayed onset — it requires 2-4 weeks of regular dosing to achieve full anxiolytic effect, similar to antidepressants. This is a critical counseling point: patients accustomed to benzodiazepines may feel buspirone 'isn't working' in the first week. Buspirone does NOT work PRN and has no cross-tolerance with benzodiazepines (won't prevent benzo withdrawal symptoms).
6. Which benzodiazepine is preferred in patients with hepatic impairment and why?
A. Diazepam — longest half-life provides stable levels
B. Lorazepam, oxazepam, or temazepam (LOT) — they undergo direct glucuronidation, NOT hepatic oxidation
C. Clonazepam — it is water-soluble
D. Alprazolam — it is renally cleared
LOT benzodiazepines (Lorazepam, Oxazepam, Temazepam) undergo direct glucuronidation — a simpler conjugation reaction less affected by hepatic disease. Other benzodiazepines (diazepam, chlordiazepoxide, alprazolam) require hepatic CYP oxidation, which is impaired in liver disease, causing drug accumulation. The mnemonic LOT helps remember which benzos are safe in liver disease.
7. Benzodiazepines produce tolerance and dependence through which mechanism?
A. Downregulation of GABA-A receptors and reduced GABA sensitivity
B. Upregulation of glutamate NMDA receptors
C. Dopamine receptor sensitization
D. Reduced serotonin synthesis
Chronic benzodiazepine use causes downregulation and desensitization of GABA-A receptors. This reduces GABAergic inhibition over time, requiring higher doses for the same effect (tolerance). On discontinuation, the compensatory increase in excitatory tone (upregulated glutamate) manifests as withdrawal: anxiety, insomnia, tremor, seizures.
8. Benzodiazepine withdrawal can be life-threatening. Which feature DISTINGUISHES it from opioid withdrawal?
A. Benzodiazepine withdrawal causes more nausea and vomiting
B. Benzodiazepine withdrawal (like alcohol withdrawal) can cause seizures and death — opioid withdrawal is rarely life-threatening
C. Opioid withdrawal causes more severe autonomic instability
D. They are equally dangerous
Benzodiazepine withdrawal (like alcohol and barbiturate withdrawal) is potentially life-threatening — severe cases cause grand mal seizures and cardiovascular collapse. This results from GABA-A downregulation leaving excitatory pathways unopposed. Opioid withdrawal, while extremely uncomfortable, is rarely life-threatening in otherwise healthy adults. This distinction drives different risk assessment and management approaches.
9. The GABA-A receptor is a ligand-gated ion channel. Benzodiazepines act as:
A. GABA mimetics — directly activating the GABA site
B. Positive allosteric modulators — binding a separate site and increasing the FREQUENCY of Cl⁻ channel opening when GABA is present
C. Negative modulators — reducing GABA activity
D. Cl⁻ channel blockers
Benzodiazepines bind to a distinct allosteric site on the GABA-A receptor (between α and γ subunits). When GABA binds, benzodiazepines potentiate the effect by increasing the FREQUENCY of chloride channel opening. They do not open the channel without GABA present. Barbiturates, by contrast, increase the DURATION of channel opening.
10. Flumazenil is the benzodiazepine antagonist. What is an important contraindication to its use?
A. Patients who are pregnant
B. Patients with chronic benzodiazepine dependence — reversal can precipitate seizures and acute withdrawal
C. Patients under 18 years of age
D. Patients with renal impairment
Flumazenil can precipitate acute severe withdrawal (including status epilepticus) in patients with chronic benzodiazepine dependence by abruptly reversing GABAergic tone. It should be used very cautiously and is NOT recommended as a routine reversal agent outside of procedural sedation in benzodiazepine-naive patients.

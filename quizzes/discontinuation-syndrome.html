Antidepressant Discontinuation Syndrome

10 questions
1. A patient who recently stopped paroxetine abruptly reports 'electric shock' sensations in her head, dizziness, and flu-like symptoms. The MOST likely diagnosis is:
A. Serotonin syndrome from residual paroxetine
B. Antidepressant discontinuation syndrome
C. Relapse of major depression
D. Medication withdrawal requiring emergency treatment
This is classic antidepressant discontinuation syndrome — 'brain zaps' (electric shock sensations) are pathognomonic. Paroxetine has the highest risk of discontinuation syndrome due to its short half-life and potent anticholinergic effects. The syndrome is NOT dangerous and NOT addiction. Treatment: restart at last dose and taper slowly, or bridge with fluoxetine.
2. FINISH is the mnemonic for antidepressant discontinuation syndrome. What does the 'S' stand for?
A. Sweating
B. Sleep disturbances
C. Sensory disturbances (brain zaps, paresthesias)
D. Suicidal thoughts
In the FINISH mnemonic: F=Flu-like, I=Insomnia, N=Nausea, I=Imbalance (dizziness), S=Sensory disturbances (electric shock/brain zaps, paresthesias, tingling), H=Hyperarousal (anxiety, agitation, irritability). Sensory disturbances — especially brain zaps — are the most distinctive and pathognomonic symptoms of discontinuation syndrome.
3. Which antidepressant has the LOWEST risk of discontinuation syndrome and can often be stopped abruptly?
A. Paroxetine
B. Venlafaxine
C. Sertraline
D. Fluoxetine
Fluoxetine and its active metabolite norfluoxetine have combined half-lives of 1-2+ weeks, creating a self-tapering effect upon discontinuation. This makes clinical discontinuation syndrome rare. Risk hierarchy: paroxetine >>> venlafaxine > sertraline, duloxetine > escitalopram, citalopram > fluoxetine (minimal). Fluoxetine is sometimes prescribed as a bridge to help patients taper off paroxetine or venlafaxine.
4. Which of the following patient/medication factors increase the risk of antidepressant discontinuation syndrome? (Select all that apply)
A. Long duration of treatment (>4 weeks)
B. Using a short half-life antidepressant
C. Abrupt discontinuation
D. Higher doses
E. Use of fluoxetine specifically
F. Female sex
Factors increasing risk: longer treatment duration (receptor adaptations), shorter half-life (faster drop in drug levels), abrupt discontinuation (vs. gradual taper), and higher doses. Fluoxetine is specifically LOW risk due to its long half-life. Sex is not a major risk factor. Prevention: always taper when possible, especially for paroxetine and venlafaxine.
5. A patient on venlafaxine 225mg/day develops severe discontinuation symptoms on every attempted taper. The MOST appropriate strategy is:
A. Continue venlafaxine indefinitely — discontinuation is not possible
B. Switch to fluoxetine at equivalent dose, then slowly taper fluoxetine over weeks to months
C. Prescribe clonidine for 2 weeks then abruptly stop venlafaxine
D. Add paroxetine to counteract the discontinuation symptoms
For patients with severe discontinuation syndrome from short-half-life agents (like venlafaxine or paroxetine), the fluoxetine bridge technique is effective: switch to equivalent fluoxetine dose, allow stabilization, then very slowly taper fluoxetine (leveraging its long half-life). Venlafaxine capsules can also be opened and beads counted for ultra-slow tapers. Simply stopping is not advisable, and paroxetine would create additional discontinuation risk.
6. Elena (45F) stopped paroxetine 10 days ago and reports brain zaps, dizziness, and flu-like symptoms. Which antidepressant has the HIGHEST risk of causing this?
A. Fluoxetine
B. Paroxetine
C. Sertraline
D. Bupropion
Paroxetine = highest discontinuation syndrome risk due to shortest half-life (~21 hours) AND anticholinergic properties. Fluoxetine = lowest risk (long half-life = self-tapering). Bupropion discontinuation causes different symptoms (seizure risk at high doses) — not typical discontinuation syndrome. FINISH mnemonic: Flu-like, Insomnia, Nausea, Imbalance, Sensory disturbances, Hyperarousal.
7. The FINISH mnemonic helps remember antidepressant discontinuation symptoms. Which symptom from the mnemonic is MOST specific (pathognomonic) to serotonergic discontinuation?
A. Flu-like symptoms
B. Insomnia
C. Sensory disturbances ('brain zaps', electric shocks)
D. Nausea
Brain zaps (sensory disturbances resembling electric shocks) are pathognomonic for serotonergic discontinuation syndrome. No other medication class causes this specific phenomenon. Flu symptoms, insomnia, and nausea can occur with many withdrawal syndromes.
8. Elena wants to stop paroxetine permanently due to side effects. The BEST tapering strategy is:
A. Stop abruptly — discontinuation syndrome is mild and self-limiting.
B. Switch to fluoxetine, then taper fluoxetine gradually.
C. Reduce by 10mg every week until zero.
D. Switch to venlafaxine, which has no discontinuation risk.
Cross-taper to fluoxetine (long half-life = self-tapering) then discontinue is the most evidence-based strategy for paroxetine discontinuation. Fluoxetine 20mg replaces paroxetine → then simply stop the fluoxetine after several weeks. Rapid tapering (10mg/week) still causes symptoms in many patients.
9. A patient who deliberately and quickly tapered their SSRI 'because I wanted to stop ASAP' comes in reporting severe discontinuation symptoms. The BEST explanation for why symptoms are so severe is:
A. They have developed physiological dependence.
B. They are experiencing serotonin syndrome from abrupt changes.
C. Abrupt discontinuation → rapid serotonin receptor upregulation → relative serotonin deficiency.
D. Antidepressants cause permanent changes to serotonin signaling.
Mechanism of discontinuation syndrome: chronic SERT inhibition causes downregulation/desensitization of serotonin receptors. Abrupt removal → rapid receptor upregulation → 'rebound' relative serotonin deficiency. This is neuroadaptation, not addiction. The distinction is clinically important for patient education.
10. Which statement about antidepressant discontinuation syndrome vs. addiction is MOST accurate?
A. Antidepressant discontinuation syndrome means the patient is addicted to the medication.
B. Antidepressant discontinuation is a neuroadaptation syndrome — not characterized by craving, drug-seeking, or dose escalation.
C. All antidepressants carry equal risk of discontinuation syndrome.
D. Discontinuation syndrome always requires hospitalization.
Antidepressant discontinuation is physiological neuroadaptation — NOT addiction. Key distinction: addiction involves craving, compulsive use, and dose escalation. Discontinuation syndrome does not. Educating patients on this distinction is critical for adherence. Risk is NOT equal across agents — paroxetine > sertraline > fluoxetine (due to half-life).

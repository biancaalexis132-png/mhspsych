Antipsychotics

Clozapine (Clozaril): The Gold Standard for TRS

10 questions
1. Treatment-resistant schizophrenia (TRS) is defined as failure of at least how many adequate antipsychotic trials from different classes?
A. 1 trial
B. 2 trials
C. 3 trials
D. 4 trials
TRS is defined as failure of ≥2 adequate antipsychotic trials from different chemical classes at adequate dose and duration (typically ≥6 weeks each at an adequate dose). Clozapine should be initiated at that point — not delayed further.
2. What is the minimum ANC (absolute neutrophil count) required before initiating clozapine?
A. ≥500/μL
B. ≥1000/μL
C. ≥1500/μL
D. ≥2500/μL
The Clozapine REMS requires a baseline ANC ≥1500/μL before initiation. If ANC drops to 1000-1499 during treatment, monitoring is increased. If ANC falls below 1000, clozapine must be stopped.
3. After the first 6 months of clozapine therapy with a stable ANC, how often must CBC be monitored?
A. Weekly
B. Every 2 weeks
C. Every 3 months
D. Annually
The REMS monitoring schedule is: weekly CBC for the first 26 weeks, then every 2 weeks for weeks 27–52, then monthly thereafter if the ANC remains stable.
4. A patient on clozapine stops smoking. What pharmacokinetic change is expected?
A. Clozapine levels decrease — dose increase needed
B. Clozapine levels increase — dose reduction may be needed
C. No significant change
D. Clozapine toxicity is unlikely due to its wide therapeutic index
Smoking induces CYP1A2, the primary metabolizing enzyme for clozapine. When a patient quits, CYP1A2 induction decreases and clozapine levels can rise by up to 50%, potentially causing toxicity (sedation, myoclonus, seizures). Monitor levels and reduce dose as needed.
5. A patient on clozapine 3 weeks into therapy develops fever, tachycardia, and troponin of 2.1 ng/mL. The MOST likely diagnosis is:
A. NMS
B. Clozapine-induced myocarditis
C. Agranulocytosis
D. Serotonin syndrome
Clozapine-induced myocarditis typically occurs in the first 4–8 weeks. Classic presentation: fever, tachycardia, elevated troponin ± CRP ± eosinophilia. Management: stop clozapine immediately and consult cardiology. This is rare (~0.1–1%) but potentially fatal.
6. What is the therapeutic serum trough level range for clozapine response?
A. 100–200 ng/mL
B. 350–600 ng/mL
C. 700–900 ng/mL
D. >1000 ng/mL
The evidence-based therapeutic target for clozapine is 350–600 ng/mL. Levels below 350 are associated with suboptimal response. Levels above 1000 ng/mL significantly increase the risk of seizures, sedation, and other toxicities.
7. Besides TRS, clozapine has a separate FDA indication for:
A. Bipolar depression
B. Suicidal behavior in schizophrenia or schizoaffective disorder
C. Augmentation of antidepressants
D. First-episode psychosis
Clozapine has two distinct FDA indications: (1) treatment-resistant schizophrenia and (2) reducing suicidal behavior in schizophrenia or schizoaffective disorder. Evidence shows it reduces suicidality even independent of overall psychosis control.
8. Fluvoxamine is added to a patient's regimen while they are taking clozapine 400 mg/day. What adjustment is required?
A. Increase the clozapine dose
B. No change needed
C. Reduce the clozapine dose — fluvoxamine inhibits CYP1A2, raising levels
D. Discontinue clozapine immediately
Fluvoxamine is a potent CYP1A2 and CYP3A4 inhibitor. It can dramatically raise clozapine plasma levels (some studies show 3–10× increase). The clozapine dose must be significantly reduced (sometimes to 1/3 of original dose) when fluvoxamine is co-prescribed.
9. Clozapine-associated constipation is clinically significant because it can progress to:
A. Acute liver failure
B. Bowel obstruction or megacolon — potentially fatal
C. Clostridium difficile colitis
D. Malabsorption syndrome
Clozapine's strong anticholinergic effects (M1 blockade) can cause severe constipation that progresses to bowel obstruction, ileus, and toxic megacolon — which has caused deaths. A prophylactic bowel regimen (stool softeners, osmotic laxatives) should be started with clozapine and maintained throughout treatment.
10. Approximately what percentage of eligible patients with TRS actually receive clozapine in clinical practice?
A. ~50%
B. ~25%
C. ~10%
D. ~2%
Despite robust evidence showing clozapine's superior efficacy for TRS, only approximately 2% of eligible patients receive it globally. Barriers include fear of agranulocytosis, REMS complexity, metabolic concerns, and prescriber/patient hesitancy. This underutilization is a major quality gap in schizophrenia care.

Mirtazapine (Remeron): The NaSSA Antidepressant

10 questions
1. Mirtazapine's mechanism is classified as:
A. SSRI
B. SNRI
C. NaSSA (Noradrenergic and Specific Serotonergic Antidepressant)
D. NDRI
Mirtazapine is classified as a NaSSA — it enhances NE and 5-HT release indirectly through α2 autoreceptor and heteroreceptor blockade (disinhibition), while selectively antagonizing 5-HT2A, 5-HT2C, and 5-HT3 receptors. This differs from SSRIs/SNRIs, which inhibit reuptake.
2. At which dose is mirtazapine MOST sedating?
A. 7.5 mg — the sedation is proportional across all doses
B. 15 mg — H1 blockade dominates at this dose
C. 30 mg — highest dose = most sedating
D. 45 mg — maximum dose
Paradoxically, mirtazapine is MOST sedating at 15 mg (the lowest standard dose) because H1 histamine blockade dominates at low concentrations. At 30 mg, increasing noradrenergic activity partially offsets H1-mediated sedation. This is the 'paradoxical dose effect' — if sedation is a problem at 15 mg, increasing to 30 mg is often LESS sedating.
3. Mirtazapine's antiemetic property comes from blockade of which receptor?
A. D2
B. H1
C. 5-HT3
D. M1
5-HT3 receptor blockade is responsible for mirtazapine's antiemetic properties — the same receptor targeted by ondansetron. This makes mirtazapine particularly useful in oncology patients (depression + chemotherapy-induced nausea), or any patient with nausea-related poor oral intake alongside depression.
4. 'California Rocket Fuel' refers to which antidepressant combination?
A. Fluoxetine + olanzapine
B. Mirtazapine + venlafaxine
C. Bupropion + sertraline
D. Mirtazapine + lithium
Mirtazapine + venlafaxine is the 'California Rocket Fuel' combination — a highly synergistic approach for TRD. Venlafaxine provides SERT + NET inhibition; mirtazapine adds α2 blockade (disinhibiting NE/5-HT release) + 5-HT2A/2C blockade. The result is maximum coverage of multiple monoamine and serotonin receptor mechanisms.
5. A patient on mirtazapine 15 mg at bedtime complains of excessive daytime sleepiness. The MOST appropriate next step is:
A. Reduce dose to 7.5 mg
B. Switch to a non-sedating antidepressant
C. Increase to 30 mg — paradoxically less sedating due to NE activation
D. Add stimulants to counteract sedation
Increasing mirtazapine from 15 mg to 30 mg often reduces daytime sedation because noradrenergic activation at higher doses partially counteracts H1-mediated sedation. This paradoxical dose-sedation relationship is a key clinical pearl for managing mirtazapine tolerability.
6. Mirtazapine is an ideal choice for which patient profile?
A. Young patient with MDD who wants to avoid weight gain
B. Patient with MDD, insomnia, anorexia, or nausea (e.g., elderly or oncology patient)
C. Patient with depression and sexual dysfunction who was previously on an SSRI
D. Patient with depression and bipolar disorder (to avoid triggering mania)
Mirtazapine's side effect profile becomes therapeutic in specific situations: strong sedation (helpful for insomnia), appetite stimulation (helpful for anorexia/cachexia in elderly or cancer patients), and antiemetic properties (5-HT3 blockade helpful for nausea). These features make it a first-choice agent in these contexts.
7. What is the usual dosing range and timing for mirtazapine?
A. 7.5–30 mg taken in the morning
B. 15–45 mg taken at bedtime (QHS)
C. 50–100 mg three times daily
D. 15 mg once weekly
Mirtazapine is almost universally dosed at bedtime (QHS) to leverage its sedating properties for sleep. Standard dosing: start 15 mg QHS, titrate to 30 mg QHS after 1–2 weeks, maximum 45 mg QHS. Dosing in the morning is rarely done as the sedation would impair daytime function.
8. Mirtazapine increases 5-HT and NE release through which mechanism?
A. Blocking SERT and NET reuptake transporters
B. Blocking α2 autoreceptors and heteroreceptors, disinhibiting NE and 5-HT release
C. Directly stimulating 5-HT1A receptors
D. Inhibiting MAO-A
Mirtazapine blocks α2 adrenergic autoreceptors (on NE neurons) and heteroreceptors (on 5-HT neurons). These are inhibitory receptors — when blocked, the 'brake' on NE and 5-HT release is released, increasing monoamine transmission. This is distinct from SSRI/SNRI mechanisms that prevent reuptake.
9. Mirtazapine's most commonly cited long-term concern is:
A. QTc prolongation
B. Anticholinergic side effects
C. Significant weight gain from 5-HT2C and H1 blockade
D. Agranulocytosis requiring CBC monitoring
Significant weight gain is mirtazapine's primary long-term concern. Both 5-HT2C blockade (appetite disinhibition) and H1 blockade (central hunger pathways) promote weight gain. Average weight gain in clinical trials is 1.5–3 kg, but long-term weight gain can be substantial. Dietary counseling and weight monitoring should accompany prescribing.
10. Regarding sexual side effects, mirtazapine:
A. Causes more sexual dysfunction than SSRIs
B. Causes equivalent sexual dysfunction to SSRIs
C. Does NOT cause serotonin-mediated sexual dysfunction due to its non-SERT mechanism
D. Causes retrograde ejaculation specifically
Mirtazapine does not inhibit serotonin reuptake, and unlike SSRIs/SNRIs, does not cause serotonin-mediated sexual dysfunction. In fact, its 5-HT2A and 5-HT3 antagonism may be beneficial for sexual function. This makes it a useful option for patients who experienced sexual dysfunction on serotonergic antidepressants.
